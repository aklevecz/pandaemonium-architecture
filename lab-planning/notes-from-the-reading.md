# Notes from the reading

Things I noticed across the syllabus that the course doesn't quite name
but keeps doing. Seven threads, meant to be read in 10–15 minutes and
then argued with. Not a summary. I'm not making the claims I'd make if
I were trying to summarize the course — I'm trying to catch some moves
the course makes across texts without unifying them, because the
unification is itself something a lab might be for.

---

## 1. The apparatus runs from geology to screen

McQuillan, Barad, Bratton, Schüll, Fogg — each names an apparatus at a
different scale. Barad's apparatus is a particle detector, a
material-discursive practice that enacts cuts. Bratton's apparatus is
the Stack: geologic layers up through protocol up through human use.
McQuillan's apparatus is AI as *"a structured structure that becomes a
structuring structure."* Schüll's apparatus is a slot machine, 1,200
parts tuned for time-on-device. Fogg's apparatus is a smart
toothbrush — he says this in 2003, matter-of-factly.

These are the same thing at different magnifications. The course
doesn't make *apparatus* the key term, and I think that's correct;
naming it too hard would be its own cut. But reading all of them
together, what surfaces is that you are never not inside an apparatus.
Bratton's Stack goes down to the mineral. Schüll's zone goes into your
skull.

The operative question for anyone making art with these tools is the
one Barad already asked in 2007: *what is the apparatus you're
committing to, and what has it already decided?*

---

## 2. There are two Duchamps in the syllabus

The Duchamp of the 1957 *Creative Act* lecture says art is a two-pole
feedback loop; the viewer refines raw material into a work. The
Duchamp of Benzel's Week 1 note is on a panel with Gregory Bateson,
who will go on to help architect the CIA's "indirect methods"; his
Vegas visit in 1963 is facilitated by the mob-connected Factor family;
1963 is also the year of JFK and the last of the CIA–mob attempts on
Castro.

Both Duchamps are real. They don't reconcile. If the creative act is
a cybernetic feedback loop, then "the spectator completes the work"
is the same *kind* of operation as an intelligence op inducing a
belief in a target population. The course refuses to let art off the
hook. It doesn't reassure you that making art with AI is clean
because Duchamp said meaning lives with the spectator. Bateson is on
the panel.

This is the right honest move and it is uncomfortable. It's why the
lab needs more than "make the viewer complete the work." It needs:
make the viewer complete the work *and* ask what kind of completion
you're inducing.

---

## 3. The reversal move has seven names

Tiqqun calls it *fog*. Vogt calls it *ritual of reversal*. Brunton
and Nissenbaum call it *obfuscation*. Colquhoun (via Fisher) calls
it *seeking the outside*. Roden calls it *subtraction*. Colebrook
calls it *shattering the globe*. Doctorow calls it *counter-
algorithmic work*. Benzel (in the *Polybius* note) calls it
*art-as-rumor*.

Seven names for one move. Two possible readings:

- **(a)** The move is so central to a left politics of technology
  that it keeps showing up independently in anthropology, philosophy,
  labor organizing, post-structuralism, science studies, and art
  criticism. The vocabulary-proliferation is evidence of convergence.
  The thing is real.

- **(b)** We don't know what to call it yet, which means we don't
  know what it is yet, which means it hasn't stabilized as a
  practice. The proliferation is evidence of vagueness.

I keep switching on which reading is right. I think both. The move is
real, but as a *practice* it has not stabilized, and the instability
is part of what a lab is for.

---

## 4. The course has a punch-line problem

Diederichsen's argument is brutal. Art compressed to a single claim
has become advertising. *Mehrwert* as payoff — the "one crucial
maneuver," the "extra inspiration," the identifiable claim — is what
art markets demand and what modernism since Duchamp has been trained
to deliver. Generative AI is the ultimate punch-line machine. Prompt
in, claim out.

But the course itself is made of punch lines. TESCREAL, shoggoth-
with-smiley-face, chickenized reverse centaurs, autoasymmetry, the
Minotaur, Weeping Angel, burbclaves, beclownment, parasites lost.
The seminar topic fields are basically a listicle, and a great one,
and the course knows this and is half-in-love with it.

This is not a contradiction I'm calling out to score a point. I
think it's the course's hardest problem and the reason the lab
needs to exist. The seminar can name TESCREAL and move on. The lab
has to make a thing that does not collapse into its name. Students
coming off two hours of great one-liners are being asked, in the
lab, to make work that resists being a one-liner. That's harder than
it sounds.

---

## 5. Fogging is political, not tactical

Week 11 pairs Brunton & Nissenbaum (obfuscation as a tool for the
weak) with the GNET Report (fogging as extremist disinformation).
Same tactic, opposite politics. The course doesn't resolve this. It
just puts them on the same syllabus line.

I think this is the deepest honest move in the course. Most
political-art curricula want their critique to be symmetrical (all
surveillance bad, all obfuscation good) or clean (good obfuscation
is democratic, bad obfuscation is authoritarian). Pandaemonium
refuses. It says: here is the tactic. Whether it is emancipation or
fascism depends on who deploys it against whom, and what they do
next.

For the lab this means students cannot get a pass by saying their
project "resists capture" or "creates opacity." Those phrases are
also what QAnon did. The reversal move is necessary but not
sufficient. The lab's real work is making students name whose side
their fog is on.

---

## 6. Weeping Angel is the image

Of all the figures the course deploys, the one that keeps coming back
for me is Weeping Angel — the CIA/GCHQ exploit that turned Samsung
Smart TVs into microphones by faking the "off" state. The screen
goes dark; the device keeps listening. Benzel pairs it with Lyotard's
"pervasive global automaton" that *"intends to eliminate all partial
impulses and immobilize bodies."*

Every AI tool students are being asked to use in the lab has a
Weeping Angel layer. You think you're not using it; it's still
running. You think the session ended; the prompt is in training. You
think you pressed delete; it's in a cache. You think you're the
artist; the model is the medium and the viewer at once.

The lab's hardest pedagogical job is making students learn to see the
angel. Not to escape it — that's Bazzell's *Extreme Privacy* move,
which works for some people some of the time. But to make work that
knows the angel is in the room.

---

## 7. Pandaemonium as a theory of thinking

Selfridge's 1958 paper gave Milton's hell-demons a new job: pattern
recognition. The demons "shriek" their detections; the decision demon
picks the loudest. John McCarthy, in the discussion that followed,
said something worth keeping:

> *If one conceives of the brain as a pandemonium — a collection of
> demons — perhaps what is going on within the demons can be regarded
> as the unconscious part of thought, and what the demons are
> publicly shouting for each other to hear, as the conscious part of
> thought.*

Take McCarthy seriously for a second. If consciousness is demons
shrieking for each other to hear, then the question the course is
asking is: *whose demons are in your head?* Which ones got there
because an apparatus loaded them? Which ones are still your own? And
when you sit at the interface and watch the shriek-hierarchy run —
the prompt goes in, the latents shuffle, the decision demon picks —
*what demon won, and why?*

The course's title is not decoration. It's a theory of mind, a
theory of AI, and a theory of what the lab is for. The lab is where
you hear which demons are yours.

---

## A closing thought for the TA (me)

Reading everything, the biggest surprise was that I kept catching the
course *doing* something it doesn't *say*. The apparatus-at-many-
scales through-line. The two-Duchamps uncomfortable pairing. The
seven-named reversal. The punch-line problem. The refusal to clean
up fogging. Weeping Angel as pervasive image. Pandaemonium as
theory-of-thinking.

I want to be careful not to name these into a new lab thesis. Part of
what the course does well is not resolving them. A good lab page
probably leaves most of these threads open, hands students the
conceptual frame without overtightening it, and trusts the readings
(or Scott's seminar) to do the actual arguing.

That said: I've now read everything. Ask me what I think and I can
tell you — the four-stance frame in `01-conceptual-frame.md` holds
up, but it's a scaffold, not the whole thing. These seven threads
are the stuff the scaffold is holding in place.
