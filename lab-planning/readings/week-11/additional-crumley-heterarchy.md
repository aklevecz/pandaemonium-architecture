## Summary

Crumley outlines *heterarchy* — systems of unranked or multiply-rankable elements — as an alternative to hierarchy for understanding social organization, power distribution, and complexity. Traces heterarchy from McCulloch's neural nets to Minsky's computer science to contemporary complexity theory, showing its ubiquity in biology, ecology, and human societies. Heterarchy explains flexibility and innovation (diversity of organizing principles) while hierarchy concentrates power. Contemporary democracies and resilient ecosystems exhibit both heterarchical and hierarchical elements.

## Key quotes

- *"Heterarchy is the relation of elements to one another when they are unranked, or when they possess the potential for being ranked in a number of different ways, depending on systemic requirements."* (line 15)
- *"Heterarchical polities receive good quality information from many sources … decisions are fair and reflect popular consensus … the society as a whole is better integrated and the workforce is proud and energized."* (line 65)
