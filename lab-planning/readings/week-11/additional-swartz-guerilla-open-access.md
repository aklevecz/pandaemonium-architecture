## Summary

Swartz's 2008 manifesto calls for the liberation of knowledge from corporate paywalls through systematic, civil-disobedient mass downloading of academic journals, scientific papers, and cultural heritage. Frames information access as a moral imperative, not a legal nicety, and advocates for copying and sharing as acts of resistance against artificial scarcity maintained by publishers.

## Key quotes

- *"Information is power. But like all power, there are those who want to keep it for themselves. The world's entire scientific and cultural heritage, published over centuries in books and journals, is increasingly being digitized and locked up by a handful of private corporations."* (line 5)
- *"There is no justice in following unjust laws. It's time to come into the light and, in the grand tradition of civil disobedience, declare our opposition to this private theft of public culture."* (line 21)
- *"With enough of us, around the world, we'll not just send a strong message opposing the privatization of knowledge — we'll make it a thing of the past."* (line 24)

## Why it's in the course

Week 11 explicitly names "Aaron Swartz's Guerilla Open Access Manifesto, JSTOR, and academic journals" as seminar material. Swartz exemplifies the activist coder working at the intersection of knowledge access, infrastructure, and civil disobedience. His death in 2013 after the JSTOR prosecution haunts the politics of open access.

## Relevance to the lab

**Reversal.** Mass downloading as performance art has precedent (Kenneth Goldsmith's *UbuWeb*, Library Genesis). Academic publishing encodes an apparatus of gatekeeping; the reversal move is exfiltration and redistribution. A project form students might take up: curate and publish an open-access version of a course reading list.
