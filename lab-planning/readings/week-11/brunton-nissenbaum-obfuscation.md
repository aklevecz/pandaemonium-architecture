## Summary

Brunton and Nissenbaum define obfuscation as "the deliberate addition of ambiguous, confusing, or misleading information to interfere with surveillance and data collection" and propose it as a small, distributed "revolution" available to those on the weak side of an information-power asymmetry — people who cannot opt out or deploy heavy cryptographic tools. Part I catalogs core cases: WWII radar chaff; Russian and Mexican Twitter bot-floods directed at protest hashtags; CacheCloak and TrackMeNot (which hide real queries inside plausible fakes); WikiLeaks' upload-spoofing scripts; false tells in poker; collective identity ("I am Spartacus," Captain Swing, the Guy Fawkes mask); identical-confederate tactics (the Craigslist robber, the *Thomas Crown Affair* crowd); excessive documentation as bureaucratic sabotage; SIM-card shuffling against NSA metadata analysis; Tor relays; babble tapes for attorney-client conversations; Operation Vula's encrypted-tape network during anti-Apartheid struggle; and shorter vignettes (orb-weaving spiders building decoys of themselves, French police decoy radar emplacements, AdNauseam's click-every-ad protest). Across the cases the authors distinguish *producing echoes* from *shuffling genuine signals* and emphasize that obfuscation's success is always context-dependent — calibrated to a specific adversary, a specific timeframe, and a specific goal (time-buying, cover, deniability, evasion, profile-interference, or protest).

## Key quotes

- *"Obfuscation is the deliberate addition of ambiguous, confusing, or misleading information to interfere with surveillance and data collection."* (line 5)
- *"obfuscation is a tool particularly well suited to the category of people without access to other modes of recourse… people who, as it happens, may be unable to deploy optimally configured privacy-protection tools because they are on the weak side of a particular information-power relationship."* (line 21)
- *"unable to refuse or deny observation, we create many plausible, ambiguous, and misleading signals within which the information we want to conceal can be lost."* (line 41)
- *"This may well be the purest, simplest example of the obfuscation approach. Because discovery of an actual airplane was inevitable… chaff taxed the time and bandwidth constraints of the discovery system by creating too many potential targets."* (line 53)
- *"obfuscation mechanisms can gain a great deal from figuring out all the parameters that can be manipulated—and from figuring out what the adversary is looking for, so as to give the adversary a manufactured version of it."* (line 139)
- *"The activities of individuals are masked by those of many ghosts, making the pattern harder to discern so that it becomes much more difficult to say of any query that it was a product of human intention rather than an automatic output of TrackMeNot."* (line 127)

## Why it's in the course

This is the practical-manual counterpart to Vogt and Tiqqun's theoretical call for zones of opacity. It gives students a working taxonomy of tactics and a design vocabulary (goals × dimensions) for making work that refuses to be cleanly read by the systems it passes through.

## Relevance to the lab

LOAD-BEARING for the **Reversal / Zones of opacity** stance. Every lab project that aims to use a tool against its native purpose — producing adversarial images, polluting a data pipeline, drowning a signal in plausible noise, building identity under a shared name — can be mapped onto Brunton and Nissenbaum's core cases. Also strongly informs **Loading the machine**: what you feed the apparatus can be chaff.
