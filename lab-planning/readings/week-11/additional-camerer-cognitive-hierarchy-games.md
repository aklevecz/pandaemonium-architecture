## Summary

The authors develop cognitive hierarchy (CH) theory, an alternative to game-theoretic Nash equilibrium. Instead of assuming all players think equally far ahead, CH models players as distributed across levels of strategic thinking (0-step thinkers randomize; k-step thinkers best-respond assuming others think 0 to k-1 steps). A single parameter (τ, the Poisson mean) characterizes the distribution. CH explains empirical deviations from equilibrium in beauty contest games, coordination games, and market entry, predicting that thinking depth ≈ 1.5 steps for most humans.

## Key quotes

- *"Players in a game are 'in equilibrium' if they are rational, and accurately predict other players' strategies. In many experiments, however, players are not in equilibrium."* (line 8)
- *"An average of 1.5 steps fits data from many games, suggesting that assuming a τ value of 1.5 could give reliable predictions for many other games as well."* (line 33)
