## Summary

Davies takes a ludological approach to QAnon, arguing that the conspiracy-movement began as (and remained structurally identical to) an Alternate Reality Game — a genre that deliberately blurs fiction and reality, hides its own gameness, and uses "rabbit holes," "breadcrumbs," and collaborative puzzle-solving to draw participants into co-creating a narrative. He situates QAnon's emergence after Gamergate within the irony-soaked /pol/ forums of 4chan/8chan, tracks the 2017 debut of the anonymous "Q" posts as one in a series of "Anon" ARGs, and follows its propagation through YouTube evangelists, Reddit, and algorithmically-driven mainstream platforms. Along the way he introduces Richard Schechner's "Dark Play" — play in which some participants don't know a game is happening — and the TINAG ("This Is Not A Game") rhetoric by which ARGs signal their own fictionality to insiders while preserving the immersion. The central thesis is that QAnon is a "dark ARG": its apophenic hermeneutics ("Do The Research") produce forensic-fandom communities whose participatory meaning-making is weaponized into political violence, culminating in the January 6th 2021 Capitol attack.

## Why it's in the course

QAnon is the most consequential demonstration of what happens when participatory fiction, recommendation algorithms, and reactionary online culture compound. For art students learning to build immersive, participatory, or transmedia work, Davies is the cautionary case study in what participatory narrative can become when the TINAG handshake breaks.

## Relevance to the lab

Primarily **Creative Act** (spectator completes the work — and can complete it into a paramilitary movement) in dialog with **Apparatus** (the platforms' recommendation engines did the evangelizing once human evangelists started the ball rolling). The chapter on "Dark Play" is an ethical limit-case for any lab project that invites audience co-creation of meaning.
