## Summary

Analyzes how extremist actors weaponize social media after terror attacks. Introduces three techniques: *fogging* (flooding information space with multiple contradictory narratives to induce epistemic confusion), *flooding* (amplifying a single false narrative through high-volume reposting across platforms), and *surfacing* (establishing plausibility through fake eyewitness claims, manipulated images, alternative sources). Case studies include Manchester Arena and Westminster Bridge attacks, showing how mis/disinformation cascades through mainstream media and radicalizes public response.

## Key quotes

- *"Fogging is an effect that arises from constructing and communicating multiple explanations and interpretations of the events in question … The effect is to create a miasma of competing and contrasting accounts and explanations in the information space, such that public audiences do not quite know what to believe happened or why, or which sources can be trusted."* (line 37)
- *"Flooding involves dominating an information space with a particular misinforming or disinforming message … making it highly visible and likely to be encountered repeatedly by audience members engaging with the event or issue of concern."* (line 39)

Week 11 seminar explicitly covers "Chaff v. Fog Reveal," "Bayesian flooding," and obfuscation. The syllabus lists "GNET Report, Fogging and Flooding" as additional reading. Operationalizes how memetic and informational attacks degrade collective sense-making.
