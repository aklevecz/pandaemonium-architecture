## Summary

Games are fundamentally an art form of agency: they sculpt temporary, disposable goals and abilities into carefully designed struggles. Unlike traditional arts (which record sights, sounds, narratives), games record *forms of agency*. The core distinction: *achievement play* (playing to win) vs. *striving play* (playing for the sake of the struggle itself). Players can "submerge" themselves in alternate agencies because game ends are temporary and transparent. This fluidity — our capacity to take up and drop different motivational structures — is what makes games educationally powerful but ethically dangerous if we gamify real life.

## Key quotes

- *"In ordinary practical life, we usually take the means for the sake of the ends. But in games, we can take up an end for the sake of the means. Playing games can be a motivational inversion of ordinary life."* (line 7)
- *"Games are a method for inscribing forms of agency into artifactual vessels: for recording them, preserving them, and passing them around."* (line 11)
- *"We have a significant capacity for agential fluidity, and games make full use of that capacity."* (line 39)
- *Suits's portable definition: "Playing a game is the voluntary attempt to overcome unnecessary obstacles."* (line 45)
- *"Games can be safely tailored precisely because they are games."* (line 167)

## Relevance to the lab

Gives the vocabulary for **Creative Act**: the player completes the game through submersion in alternate agency. Also informs the lab's project ethic: a student whose AI piece "succeeds" at its prompt is an *achievement player*; a student who imposes unnecessary obstacles on the tool is a *striving player*. The lab wants the second. Useful also in Week 6 (game engines) and Week 11 (gamification of conspiracy). Warning: Nguyen is also clear about the danger of gamifying moral life — important to keep as a brake on "everything is a game" readings.
