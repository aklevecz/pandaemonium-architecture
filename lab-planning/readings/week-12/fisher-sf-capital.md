## Summary

Fisher uses *2001: A Space Odyssey* vs. *Star Wars* to trace how late capitalism colonized desire, branding, and everyday experience. Where *2001* depicted a satirically managed corporate future that now appears banal (our actual present), *Star Wars* pioneered the "hyper-commodity" — not a discrete product but an entire fictional universe designed for endless exploitation. Capital itself is purely virtual, a feedback loop of finance and speculation with no grounding in use value. The 1960s counterculture became the generation of billionaire tech entrepreneurs who now administer total control systems. "SF Capital" is science-fiction capital: the future as marketable universe.

## Key quotes

- *"Star Wars is metonymically implicated in late capitalism in a way that 2001 never quite could be. What was bought and sold when audiences consumed Star Wars was not in any sense a single (aesthetic) object, but a world, a hype(r)verse."* (line 53)
- *"The hype-commodity is not an object, but an intricate, microsensitive, semiotic web, inducing participation and 'involvement.'"* (line 85)
- *"Capital's apparent orientation towards the future, meanwhile, is 'speculative' only in the sense that it is immediately efficient."* (line 63)

## Why it's in the course

Week 12's seminar is *Asymmetrical Likewar to Autoasymmetry*: Psyop to Mindwar to Likewar, SF Capital, hype-vorticism, beclownment, rewiring social structure. Fisher supplies the historical-economic backbone — how apparatuses colonize consciousness; how satire becomes indistinguishable from reality. Anticipates the bind students face: critique of algorithmic control already sounds like marketing.

## Relevance to the lab

**Apparatus.** Fisher models how to name the tool's prior decisions — how *Star Wars* pre-packaged desire as commodity. Students making art with/against AI need Fisher's framework to recognize they're already inside the feedback loop. The hype-commodity concept is useful for thinking about generative AI tools themselves as hype-commodities — the tool sells you a world of possibilities, not a feature.
