# Marcel Duchamp, *The Creative Act* (1957)

**Week:** 1 (Additional / Primary Documents)
**File:** `markdown/additional_reading_primary_documents/Marcel Duchamp, The Creative Act.md`
**Length:** 42 lines (short — under 2 pages)

## Summary

Transcript of Duchamp's April 1957 talk at the American Federation of Arts
in Houston (on a panel with Rudolf Arnheim and Gregory Bateson). The talk
introduces the "art coefficient" and the claim that the artist is a
"mediumistic being" who cannot self-interpret. Art is completed by the
spectator, not by the artist, and "in the last analysis, the artist may
shout from all the rooftops that he is a genius; he will have to wait for
the verdict of the spectator." Short enough to assign in full; foundational
for every other reading in the course that talks about art-making.

## Key quotes

- *"Let us consider two important factors, the two poles of the creation
  of art: the artist on one hand, and on the other the spectator who
  later becomes the posterity."* (line 3)
- *"All his decisions in the artistic execution of the work rest with
  pure intuition and cannot be translated into a self-analysis, spoken or
  written, or even thought out."* (line 7)
- *"The personal 'art coefficient' is like an arithmetical relation
  between the unexpressed but intended and the unintentionally
  expressed."* (line 35)
- *"This 'art coefficient' is a personal expression of art 'à l'état
  brut,' that is, still in a raw state, which must be 'refined' as pure
  sugar from molasses, by the spectator."* (line 37)
- *"The creative act is not performed by the artist alone; the spectator
  brings the work in contact with the external world by deciphering and
  interpreting its inner qualifications and thus adds his contribution
  to the creative act."* (line 43)

## Why it's in the course

Week 1 pairs it with Jacques (cybersemiotics) and Benzel's own notes on
Duchamp/cybernetics. The panel itself included Gregory Bateson — Duchamp
was already in the cybernetic conversation in 1957. The course uses this
text as the ur-theory of art-making: art as a two-pole feedback loop.
