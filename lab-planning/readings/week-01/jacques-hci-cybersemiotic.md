## Summary

Jacques proposes a *cybersemiotic* framework for human-computer interaction that transcends traditional HCI models. Rather than treating the user–information–interface triad as separate mediating elements, the cybersemiotic experience integrates four co-equal mediators: user, information, interface, and *context* (external circumstances). Consciousness expands through the semiotic dance of these elements operating simultaneously from "outside" (active observation, linear dynamics) and "inside" (participation, non-linear embodied processes). Jacques draws on Peirce, von Uexküll, and cybernetic theory to argue that interactive hybrid environments can generate meaning through mediated exchange rather than unidirectional information transmission.

## Key quotes

- *"In the creation of meaning, the possibility of new mediations and perceptions reflects the semiotic dance and the infinite possibility of interactions in meaning production."* (line 445)
- *"To expand consciousness within interactive hybrid environments, it is necessary to ensure that user and information are also being seen as mediators in the system."* (line 406)
- *"The Cybersemiotic Experience expands Goodman's aims beyond the artistic realm and into the information processing paradigm by attempting to reconcile: human meaningful information with meaningfully algorithmic processing of information."* (lines 430–432)
- *"User, information, and interface can then be seen taking turns in mediating the interaction and promoting meaning through an inner-outer world exchange."* (line 441)

## Why it's in the course

Week 1 pairs this with Benzel's À bruit secret notes. Jacques reads Duchamp's *Creative Act* cybernetically — she explicitly cites Duchamp as "the foundation of any and every human-computer interaction." The reading establishes that no tool is neutral and that meaning-making is baked into the apparatus.

## Relevance to the lab

Primarily informs **Apparatus** and **Creative Act**. The cybersemiotic model demonstrates how the spectator (user) completes the work by engaging in a feedback loop of meaning-making with the system — not by consuming it. This is crucial for understanding how feedback loops between maker and apparatus differ from unidirectional "prompting."
