## Summary

Benzel traces a genealogy from the ancient historian Polybius (who insisted on facticity against popular mythology) through the mythic/vaporware 1981 arcade game *Polybius* (RumINT circulating on gaming websites for years) to *Nitro Zeus*, a massive NSA cyberwar program disclosed anonymously in the 2016 documentary *Zero Days*, whose actual scale and targets remain ambiguous. *Art-as-rumor* — work that circulates as legend, vaporware, or unverifiable claim — is a legitimate artistic stance. The game *Polybius* became iconic precisely because it may not exist: kids allegedly suffered amnesia after playing it; government agents in black coats collected records; the ROM eventually surfaced online, reproducible but never fully credible. Nitro Zeus similarly circulates as both possible cyberwar capability and PSYOP. In an era of military memetics and necropolitical information warfare, making work that does not definitively exist — that exists only as rumor, as alleged, as semiotic presence without material referent — is a form of counter-practice.

## Key quotes

- *"In tragedy the plausible takes precedence, even if it is false so as to deceive the spectators, while in history the truth takes precedence to benefit the hearer."* (Polybius epigraph, lines 23–24)
- *"This game had a very limited release, one or two backwater arcades in a suburb of Portland. The history of this game is cloudy — there were all kinds of strange stories about how kids who played it got amnesia afterwards, couldn't remember their name or where they lived."* (coinop.org, lines 167–169)
- *"We spent hundreds of millions, maybe billions on it … We were inside, waiting, watching. Ready to disrupt, degrade, and destroy those systems with cyber attacks. In comparison, Stuxnet was a back alley operation. [Nitro Zeus] was the plan for a full scale cyber war with no attribution."* (anonymous NSA source in *Zero Days*, lines 317–318)
- *"Such figures of sovereignty are far from a piece of prodigious insanity or an expression of a rupture between the impulses and interests of the body and those of the mind. Indeed, they, like the death camps, are what constitute the nomos of the political space in which we still live."* (Mbembe, *Necropolitics*, lines 282–283)
- *"As Polybius of Arcadia, captive of Rome, champion of facticity and 'universal history' against tragedy and spectacle, fades, his namesake game, one (of many) early vector overwhelming facticity with spectacular mimesis and incipient necropolitics, comes under a new light."* (lines 479–482)

## Why it's in the course

Week 5's seminar covers *Young Slime Life* (swarm, agent, assemblage) and Polybius/Nitro Zeus (psyop, rumortech, vaporware, DARPA SMISC/INCAS, GCHQ JTRIG). This note is Benzel's most direct argument for a specific aesthetic posture: art that operates in the informational/memorial register state agencies already occupy.

## Relevance to the lab

Opens **Reversal / Zones of opacity** fully. Students might make work that refuses to stabilize as fact — work circulating as RumINT, alleged, as legend or vaporware. Not deception in service of the apparatus, but mimicking the apparatus's own tactics of ambiguity to resist clarification and capture. Polybius is a template: a project that may or may not exist, whose history is "cloudy," whose effects circulate as rumor. Possible project forms include: a game never fully released; a project that *performs* its own declassification; a work using aesthetics of military memetics to induce uncertainty in the spectator.
