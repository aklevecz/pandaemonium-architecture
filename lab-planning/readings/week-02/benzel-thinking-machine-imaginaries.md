## Summary

Benzel traces a lineage of *recombinatory imaginaries* — machines that generate meaning by combining pre-existing elements according to formal rules — from Ramon Llull's 13th-century wheels through Leibniz's binary calculus and Ada Lovelace's algorithmic notation to contemporary AI and the "Shoggoth with a Smiley Face" meme. The key insight: what gets *loaded into* the machine determines what it produces. Borges's 1937 essay on Llull captures this: Llull fed his combinatorial diagrams with theological attributes (goodness, power, wisdom); we could load them with "Entropy, Time, Electrons" or "Surplus Value, Proletariat, Capitalism." The machine itself is substrate-agnostic. Lovelace's Note A — *"it might act upon other things besides number"* — opens the door to symbolic computing and AI a century early. The note ends on the Shoggoth meme: a pedagogical analog for the opacity of contemporary neural networks, spreading through affective resonance rather than explanation.

## Key quotes

- *"We (who are basically no less naive than Llull) would load the machine differently, no doubt with the words Entropy, Time, Electrons, Potential Energy, Fourth Dimension, Relativity, Protons, Einstein. Or with Surplus Value, Proletariat, Capitalism, Class Struggle, Dialectical Materialism, Engels."* (Borges on Llull, lines 133–136)
- *"The idea of a thinking machine draws from the tradition of the ancient zairja … the main aim was not to clarify thought but to confuse it. Combinatorial diagrams cross-pollinated ideas so that their inner forms became visible."* (Libeskind on zairja, lines 154–168)
- *"…it might act upon other things besides number, were objects found whose mutual relations could be expressed by those of the abstract science of operations … the engine might compose elaborate and scientific pieces of music of any degree of complexity or extent."* (Lovelace, Note A, lines 644–651)
- *"The ars is a ladder for ascending and descending; as, for instance, descending from a completely general principle to one neither completely general nor completely particular…"* (Llull, *Ars Brevis*, lines 266–270)
- *"Accompanied by this triple caption, the image has a stronger pedagogic dimension than previous versions of the analogy… This sudden notoriety arguably contributed to an aesthetic and semantic taming of an analogy which might at first appear as obscure."* (Goujon & Ricci on Shoggoth meme, lines 837–856)

## Why it's in the course

Week 2's seminar is *Shoggoth with a Smiley Face*: Llull to Leibniz to Lovecraft; the Black Box; Technocracy, Inc.; Claude Shannon; information theory; basilisks; antimemetics. This note is Benzel's spine for the week — braiding the historical genealogy of thinking machines with the contemporary Shoggoth meme to argue that what matters is not the machine's elegance but *what you feed it*.

## Relevance to the lab

This note is the *engine* of the **Loading the Machine** stance. Gives students permission — and a genealogy — to think of their artistic practice as choosing what to feed the apparatus. Borges's move is explicit: the creative act is not in building the machine but in *deciding what concepts, words, materials, or rules you'll constrain it with*. Also bridges to **Apparatus**: Lovelace's realization that machines operate on *concepts*, not just numbers, means all machines are already cultural machines.
