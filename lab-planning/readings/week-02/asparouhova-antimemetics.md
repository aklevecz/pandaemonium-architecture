## Summary

Applies memetic theory to explain why some ideas spread easily (memes), some consume attention (supermemes), and some actively resist spreading (antimemes). Antimemes are high-impact, cognitively expensive ideas that individuals and networks unconsciously filter out: uncomfortable truths, taboos, long-term planning, moral dilemmas. Asparouhova uses the Galapagos Islands as metaphor — isolated group chats and private online spaces function as "memetic Galapagos" where ideas mutate rapidly in dense networks. Attention is the mechanism governing spread; selective attention is both a survival skill and a source of collective blindness.

## Key quotes

- *"Dense, isolated networks appear to be more stable and harmless, but they have weak immune systems."* (line 14)
- *"Group chats are like social islands. As fast as the internet's public highways might be, ideas evolve even more rapidly in private online environments."* (line 22)
- *"Antimemes are highly consequential ideas that face strong resistance from the nodes in a network. Like fiber, they move slowly through the system and take time to digest."* (lines 63–64)
- *"Supermemes are like black holes. Like memes, they spread quickly, but unlike memes, they are perceived as highly consequential… whereas antimemes are characterized by a 'strange forgetting' by the perceiver, supermemes are characterized by a 'strange inability to forget.'"* (line 76)
- *"Our antimemetic motives loom large in our minds: the eponymous 'elephant in the brain,' silently guiding our choices."* (line 205)

Bridges qntm's fiction with real-world cognitive biases: forgetting, opacity, resistance are not supernatural but structural to how minds and networks function.
