## Summary

A former FBI Cyber Crimes Task Force investigator (and technical consultant on *Mr. Robot* season 1) writes the operational manual for going invisible in the contemporary data environment. The book is strictly procedural: ghost addresses, PMBs in South Dakota and Texas, nomad residency, LLCs and trusts holding cars and homes, anonymous utilities, Faraday bags, pfSense firewalls, alias wallets, Signal configuration, burner phones, and disinformation seeding. Bazzell's four archetypal clients — the wealthy executive, the celebrity, the government employee, and "usually a woman" fleeing a violent ex — frame privacy as physical safety first, ideology second. Maslow repurposed: *"food, shelter, and love"* becomes *"anonymous purchasing options, a ghost address, and a clean alias."*

## Why it's in the course

Week 2 pairs Bazzell with the Andreessen manifesto and the Grosser redaction: three positions on the same apparatus of platform capture. Where Andreessen evangelizes the techno-capital machine and Grosser redacts it, Bazzell simply *exits*. The book is also a primary source for the Week 2 *Antimemetics* arc — "self-classifying" information, *How to disappear completely*, "extreme privacy and its discontents" — and the Week 11 *Obfuscation* reading (Brunton and Nissenbaum) builds on exactly this tradition.

## Relevance to the lab

**Reversal / Zones of opacity (iv), with stance (i) in the frame.** Bazzell is the most concrete available manual for stance (iv): each step (PMB, LLC-owned car, Privacy.com alias card, seeded disinformation) is a rewiring of a surveillance apparatus against its native purpose of identification. The book also enforces stance (i) by negation — the reader learns what the apparatus has already decided about them only by trying to extract themselves from it.
