## Summary

Fogg's 2003 founding text of "captology" (Computers As Persuasive Technologies) is the academic license plate of the Stanford Persuasive Technology Lab whose alumni went on to design TikTok, Instagram, and the rest of the contemporary attention economy. The Preface and Introduction lay out the research program: computers are now "interactive computing systems designed to change people's attitudes and behaviors," and they have six characteristic advantages over human persuaders — they are persistent, anonymous, can manage huge data, use many modalities, scale infinitely, and are ubiquitous (even where humans wouldn't be welcome). Fogg presents this as ethically neutral terrain to be developed responsibly, while volunteering examples (BARN, Quitnet, "Alcohol 101," embedded car systems, smart toothbrushes) that anticipate the entire behavior-design playbook.

## Key quotes

- *"When I was 10 years old, I studied propaganda. Each week my fifth grade class would meet with a teaching intern from Fresno State University. He showed us how the media and politicians use techniques to change what people think and do."* (line 7)
- *"I define persuasive technology as any interactive computing system designed to change people's attitudes or behaviors."* (line 87)
- *"No human can be as persistent as a machine. Computers don't get tired, discouraged, or frustrated. They don't need to eat or sleep. They can work around the clock in active efforts to persuade, or watch and wait for the right moment to intervene."* (line 247)
- *"With the emergence of embedded computing, the forms of persuasive technology will likely become more diverse, 'invisible,' and better integrated into everyday life."* (lines 111–115)
- *"With the growth of embedded computers, computing applications are becoming commonplace in locations where human persuaders would not be welcomed, such as the bathroom or bedroom, or where humans cannot go (inside clothing, embedded in an automotive system, or implanted in a toothbrush)."* (line 291)

## Why it's in the course

Fogg is the genealogical document of the persuasive-design industry. Reading the source — written in genteel academic prose, full of "ethics chapter forthcoming" disclaimers — makes visible how matter-of-factly the field was built around influencing users without their consent, and gives students the original vocabulary of "captology," "ubiquity," and "persistence" that engagement-driven products still operationalize.

## Relevance to the lab

Apparatus: Fogg's six advantages list reads as a specification sheet for the very behaviors the apparatus has *already decided* on by the time anyone uses an app — persistence, anonymity, data, modality, scale, ubiquity. Reversal: the lab can pair this reading with Brunton & Nissenbaum to design exercises that turn captology's tactics back against the captologists.
