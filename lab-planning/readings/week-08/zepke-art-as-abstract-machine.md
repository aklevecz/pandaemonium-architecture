## Summary

Zepke outlines Deleuze and Guattari's onto-aesthetics: the abstract machine as a non-representational, creative generator of new realities rather than a mirror of existing ones. Art, as abstract machine, does not represent the world but *constructs* it — an immanent, material practice that expresses and creates simultaneously. Weaves Nietzsche (affirmation, will to power), Spinoza (immanence, affect), and Bergson (creative duration) into a framework where aesthetics and ontology become indissociable. The abstract machine is a "cosmic artisan" that builds new modes of existence; art is inseparable from ethics and politics because it experiments with and materializes new possibilities for life itself.

## Key quotes

- *"The diagrammatic or abstract machine does not function to represent, even something real, but rather constructs a real that is yet to come, a new type of reality."*
- *"Aesthetics instead involves the determination of real conditions that are no wider than the experience itself, that are, once more, indiscernible from this experience. Aesthetics then, is inseparable from ontology, because experience is, for Deleuze and Guattari, irreducibly real."*
- *"No creation without destruction. A motto for the artist first of all."*
- *"Art is, before all else, and as Deleuze and Guattari put it, a sensation. A sensation of this work, but this work, this sensation, it does nothing if it does not restore us to our constitutive infinity by creating the world anew."*
- *"Building an abstract machine is more DIY than techno-science, and requires a bit of the mad professor."*

## Why it's in the course

Week 8 is titled "Art as Abstract Machine" and is paired with Goodfellow on GANs and Fogg on persuasive computing. The seminar uses Zepke to make the strongest available claim about what art *is* in relation to the course's machines: not representation, not critique, but construction of new realities. The week is the philosophical midpoint and re-anchor for the project arc.

## Relevance to the lab

All four stances converge here: **Apparatus** (the abstract machine names what the apparatus decides), **Creative Act** (the feedback loop between maker and world is generative), **Loading the machine** (artistic choices about parameters and data), **Reversal** (using the apparatus to create new realities, not merely reproduce). Caveat: Zepke's vocabulary (becoming, immanence, atheist mysticism, deterritorialization) is demanding. For students without prior Deleuze, the lab should not require this reading — Duchamp's *Creative Act* covers adjacent ground in accessible prose.
