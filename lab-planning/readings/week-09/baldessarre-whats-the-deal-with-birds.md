## Summary

This is a deliberate academic hoax. Baldassarre, a real ornithologist, submitted a paper written in absurd Seinfeld-register prose ("Birds are pretty weird. I mean, they have feathers. WTF?") to the *Scientific Journal of Research and Reviews*, a predatory open-access journal, and it was accepted and published in 2020. The "argument" — that birds are weird, that he looked at a woodpecker, a parrot, and a penguin, that a penguin "looked more like a fish!" — is self-evidently nonsense, but the methods section and reference list are salted with real citations to the author's own legitimate fairy-wren research, and the tables present real repeatability statistics from prior studies relabeled "Weird birds / WTF." The piece *is* the hoax: the legible content is bait, and the actual payload is a demonstration that pay-to-publish journals will rubber-stamp anything.

## Why it's in the course

The paper is a working example of adversarial content-making: a real scientist uses the vocabulary and apparatus of peer-reviewed science to expose a specific infrastructure (predatory journals) by feeding it something that should be rejected and watching it get indexed with a DOI anyway. The "argument" is not in the text; the argument is the publication event.

## Relevance to the lab

Strongly informs **Loading the machine** and **Reversal/Opacity**. What the artist feeds the apparatus (a lisping joke dressed as a research article) is what the artist's choice *is*; the hoax also demonstrates how to use a legitimating system against its own purpose, producing an artifact that is legible as "science" to an automated pipeline and illegible as science to a human.
