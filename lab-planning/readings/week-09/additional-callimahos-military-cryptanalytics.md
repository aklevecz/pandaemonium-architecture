## Summary

This is the 1977 NSA textbook by Lambros Callimahos, declassified in 2020 in response to a 2012 FOIA request. The portion read (front matter, Chapter I, and selected passages) lays out the science of attacking aperiodic substitution systems — ciphers designed to defeat the cryptanalyst's first move (counting alphabets, factoring intervals) by varying either key length, plaintext-grouping length, or both. Callimahos writes in a wry, almost belletristic register, opening with Shakespeare and footnotes about violinists and Fortuna; he treats cryptanalysis as both an exact science and a literary craft. The included chapter on "cryptodiagnosis" frames the discipline as one of attentively reading an unknown system into legibility, and the FOIA preface itself documents the apparatus of partial declassification under Executive Order 13526.

## Why it's in the course

Callimahos is the NSA's own self-portrait of the cryptanalytic mind: patient, technical, literary, opportunistic, faintly amused. The FOIA letter at the front is itself a primary document of the apparatus that decides what students of cryptography are even allowed to see. For a seminar that pairs cybernetics with the surveillance state, this is the genuine NSA pedagogical voice rather than a critique of it.

## Relevance to the lab

Apparatus: the redactions and the "exempt from automatic declassification" language are an apparatus naming itself. Reversal: Callimahos's whole stance — solve the system that was designed to be unsolvable, "discover and exploit these elements" — is a textbook (literally) of using the tool against its native purpose, and is the model the syllabus draws on when it pairs cryptanalysis with Tiqqun and Brunton/Nissenbaum.
