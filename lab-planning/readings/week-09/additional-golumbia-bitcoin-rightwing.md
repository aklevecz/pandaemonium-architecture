## Summary

Golumbia's short 2016 book argues that Bitcoin's economic and political assumptions — central banking as theft, fiat currency as conspiracy, "tyranny" as any government, the gold standard as protection against an imminent collapse — were almost entirely produced by the U.S. far right (Liberty League, John Birch Society, Tea Party, Mises Institute, Rothbard, Ron Paul) and that "cyberlibertarian" technologists adopted them, often unknowingly, by adopting the right's redefinitions of "freedom," "government," and "tyranny." He situates this within Mirowski's account of the Mont Pelerin Society/neoliberalism and shows that Bitcoin fails the textbook three-function definition of money (medium of exchange, store of value, unit of account) precisely because its volatility serves its actual function as a speculative commodity for already-existing capital.

## Key quotes

- *"Bitcoin was said to be different. Its difference stemmed from at least two sources: first, that it was based on a relatively new form of cryptographic software technology called 'blockchain,' and second, that throughout 2013 Bitcoin had skyrocketed in its value relative to official world currencies"* (line 7)
- *"Dogma propagated almost exclusively by far-right groups like the Liberty League, the John Birch Society, the militia movement, and the Tea Party, conspiracy theorists like Alex Jones and David Icke, and to a lesser extent rightist outlets like the Fox media group and some right-wing politicians, was now being repeated by many who seemed not to know the origin of the ideas, or the functions of those ideas in contemporary politics."* (line 7)
- *"At its most expansive, cyberlibertarianism can be thought of as something like a belief according to which freedom will emerge inherently from the increasing development of digital technology, and therefore entails that efforts to interfere with or regulate that development must be antithetical to freedom"* (line 11)
- *"Bitcoin and the blockchain technology on which it rests satisfy needs that make sense only in the context of right-wing politics; those of us who do not share those politics must, therefore, view Bitcoin and the blockchain with both skepticism and a clear eye for the political terms and concepts invoked in the discourse surrounding them."* (line 27)
- *"In this sense, it becomes a tool for existing power to concentrate itself, rather than a challenge to the existing order: as some better economically informed commentators consistently point out, Bitcoin functions much more like a speculative investment than a currency"* (lines 43–45)

## Why it's in the course

Golumbia provides the cleanest, shortest available argument that the cryptocurrency thread of "Web3" is not politically neutral software but the operationalization of a specific right-wing economics. For a seminar with Tiqqun, Brunton & Nissenbaum, and a cybernetic-surveillance week, it gives students an analytical vocabulary for unmasking the politics buried inside libertarian-coded technology.

## Relevance to the lab

Apparatus: Golumbia is the model exercise of "name what your tool decided before you arrived" — Bitcoin's volatility, deregulation, and gold-fetishism are presented as features by people who do not realize they are repeating Murray Rothbard. Useful pre-reading for any lab unit that touches blockchain, NFTs, or "decentralization" rhetoric.
