## Summary

DeLanda's introduction traces the concept of *emergence* from 19th-century philosophy through contemporary complexity science. Emergent properties (temperature, convection, thunderstorms) require explanation not of the properties themselves but of the *mechanisms* that produce them and the *possibility spaces* that structure them. Simulation becomes a research tool: computational models can instantiate mechanisms that generate emergent effects, making emergence experimentally tractable. Sketches examples from physics (temperature, pressure, convection cells) to argue for a materialist philosophy freed from both mysticism and reductionism.

## Key quotes

- *"The early emergentists dismissed this idea because they could not imagine anything more complex than a linear clockwork mechanism. But there are many other physical mechanisms that are nonlinear."* (line 11)
- *"The objective reality of emergent properties can be established by elucidating the mechanisms that produce them at one scale and by showing that emergent entities at that scale can become the component parts of a whole at a larger scale."* (line 59)

## Why it's in the course

Week 3 seminar explicitly names DeLanda alongside Philip K. Dick, Fredric Jameson, and Lyotard in its "Simulationism in Art" thread. Grounds the course's engagement with simulation as both epistemic tool and ontological claim. Agent-based modeling and generative systems derive from this philosophy.

## Relevance to the lab

**Apparatus**, **Creative Act**, **Loading**, **Reversal** — all four. Simulation encodes assumptions about mechanisms; what emerges unexpectedly in simulation is a form of creative act; what mechanisms and initial conditions generate desired outcomes is Loading; and using simulation against its intended purpose (simulate systemic collapse rather than optimization) is Reversal. DeLanda's framework is a good generic theory for students working with any generative or agent-based system.
