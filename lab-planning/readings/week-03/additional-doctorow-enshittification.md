**Length:** 332 lines

## Summary

A short column built around one discovery. Doctorow tries Kagi, a paid search engine that his friends the Nielsen Haydens recommend, and finds it gives him "that same feeling" 1998 Google did. Then — via Jason Koebler in 404 Media — he realizes Kagi is largely "a heavily customized, anonymized front-end to Google" (with Yandex, Mojeek, Brave, and its own index mixed in). The conclusion: Google's degraded results aren't a technical limitation but a business choice. The essay introduces FTC chair Lina Khan's phrase *"too big to care"* as the counterpart to "too big to fail" — monopolists enshittify not because they can't do better, but because indifference is the most profitable posture once lock-in is absolute.

## Key quotes

- *"Google is shifting value away from end users (searchers) and business customers (advertisers, publishers and merchants) to itself… This is enshittification."* (lines 47–49)
- *"Kagi is a heavily customized, anonymized front-end to Google."* (line 113)
- *"The implications of this are stunning. It means that Google's enshittified search-results are a choice. Those ad-strewn, sub-Altavista, spam-drowned search pages are a feature, not a bug. Google prefers those results to Kagi, because Google makes more money out of shit than they would out of delivering a good product."* (line 115)
- *"One of my favorite TV comedy bits is Lily Tomlin as Ernestine the AT&T operator; Tomlin would do these pitches for the Bell System and end every ad with 'We don't care. We don't have to. We're the phone company'."* (line 121)
- *"Google used to care. They cared because they were worried about competitors and regulators. They cared because their workers made them care… Google doesn't care anymore. They don't have to. They're the search company."* (lines 149–153)

## Relevance to the lab

**Apparatus (i), with a side of Reversal (iv).** The Kagi revelation is a textbook stance-(i) moment: the tool had already decided, before you arrived, to rank spam above the answer you wanted. The piece also models stance (iv) in microcosm — Kagi is Google's own index used against Google's own monetization design. A good lab prompt: "find a second apparatus whose native results you can reroute through a different scoring function."
