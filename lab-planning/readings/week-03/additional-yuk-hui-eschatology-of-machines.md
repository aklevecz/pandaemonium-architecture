## Summary

Hui's 2023 e-flux essay argues that the panic and ecstasy around ChatGPT are both symptoms of an outdated, mechanistic stereotype of machines inherited from eighteenth-century philosophy. Drawing on Simondon, Günther, and Brian Cantwell Smith, he insists that contemporary ML systems are *recursive* (cybernetic, reflective) and not merely linear, and that the right question is not "can machines think?" but how to develop "an adequate culture of prosthesis" — a use of machines that liberates rather than replaces human capacity. He closes with three premises: suspend anthropomorphic stereotyping, integrate machines into a diversity of human realities, and refuse the apocalyptic philosophy of history that frames the AI debate.

## Key quotes

- *"Such a syntactical critique is based on a mechanistic epistemology that assumes linear causality—a cause followed by an effect... In contrast to linear causality and mechanist philosophy, the eighteenth century saw the rise of philosophical thinking based on the organism, with Immanuel Kant's Critique of Judgment as one of the most significant contributions."* (line 43)
- *"Recursive machines, and not linear machines, are key to understanding the development and evolution of artificial intelligence."* (line 59)
- *"In developing countries, a new type of cheap labor employs humans to tells machines whether results are correct, be they facial recognition scans or ChatGPT responses. This new form of labor, which exploits workers who toil invisibly behind the machines we interact with, is often overlooked by very general criticisms of capitalism that lament insufficient automation."* (lines 65–71)
- *"We live in various positive feedback loops represented as culture... In the past, philosophy was tasked with limiting the hubris produced by machines and with freeing human subjects from feedback loops in the name of truth. Today, philosophers of technology are instead anxious to affirm these feedback loops as the inevitable path of civilization."* (line 81)
- *"Instead of suspending the development of AI, suspend the anthropomorphic stereotyping of machines and develop an adequate culture of prosthesis."* (line 93)

## Why it's in the course

Hui gives a usable philosophical frame for the chatbot moment without either techno-utopianism or apocalypticism. He insists on the distinction between mechanism and organism/recursion that the syllabus's cybernetics and Simondon thread depends on, and he names the invisible labor and feedback-loop culture students are about to plug their own work into.

## Relevance to the lab

Apparatus: Hui's discussion of click-work labor and the industry's "self-fulfilling prophecy of replacement" is exactly the prior-decided field students must name. Reversal / Zones of opacity: his three closing premises read as instructions for using AI as prosthesis rather than replacement — a productive lab prompt for assignments that refuse the chatbot's intended productivity-enhancement use case.
