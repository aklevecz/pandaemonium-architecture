## Summary

Benzel explores how genres and meaning-structures are *retrospectively assembled* by fan communities and archivists, not declared by creators or institutions. Primary example: Dungeon Synth, a microgenre that did not exist as a named category until 2011, when fan Andrew Werdna created "The Dungeon Synth Blog" to curate and connect music released decades earlier (Kirkwood's *Master of Dragons*, 1991; Mortiis's *Født til å herske*, 1994). Parallels this with the Mandela Effect: shared false memories circulating online, often involving logos, film quotes, biblical passages. Both phenomena are *retrospective assemblages*. Students need not know what their work "is" before making it; meaning and category will be determined later by how audiences connect, recontextualize, and archive the work.

## Key quotes

- *"Classic dungeon synth just existed collectively and independently in the mind of musicians with their releases, which were then resurrected by fans and archivists decades after their original releases. Before 2011, dungeon synth as a concept and even name did not exist … rather it was a nebula of likeminded music."* (lines 171–173)
- *"Dungeon synth, in its classic sense, exists due to the will of its fans who are uncovering more and more releases, which in turn, reshapes its history over and over."* (lines 175–176)
- *"Most genres and microgenres are, to greater or lesser degrees, retrospective: diverse works are grouped together by fans and critics based on shared formal and concept characteristics."* (lines 37–38)
- *"The Mandela Effect is an internet phenomenon describing shared and consistent false memories for specific icons in popular culture."* (Prasad & Bainbridge, lines 95–97)
- *"As we retrospectively reconfigure or attempt to reclaim the past, new assemblages come into being while certainties are lost … Are we not ourselves retrospectively made? Do we not ourselves lose who we were or thought we were?"* (from earlier read)

## Why it's in the course

Week 3's seminar covers enshittification, Baudrillard's evil demon, AI slopworld, and the (a)social (de)construction of reality. Benzel uses Dungeon Synth and the Mandela Effect as twin examples of how digital culture enables asynchronous, distributed meaning-making. Both show the past is constantly being rewritten by new assemblages.

## Relevance to the lab

Liberates students from the burden of *knowing what they're making*. Artistic practice can be generative in the dark — work made without genre-consciousness, later recognized and reshaped by audiences. Complements **Creative Act** (the spectator/archivist completes the work) and **Apparatus** (archives, blogs, and platforms decide what becomes a genre). A student project might deliberately refuse to stabilize, existing as rumor or as a work that circulates before it solidifies.
