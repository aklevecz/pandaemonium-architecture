## Summary

Images no longer *represent* reality; they *precede and generate* it. The image is "most diabolical" precisely when it appears most faithful and realistic — its "evil" is that it looks like it's showing you the world while actually *replacing* the world with a simulacrum. Drawing on Cartesian doubt and Manichaeism, Baudrillard traces images from mirrors (Renaissance) to models (industrial modernity) to *hyperreality* (media age), where the referent disappears entirely. Vietnam and *Apocalypse Now* exemplify this — the film is not a representation of war but war's continuation by cinematic means. *The China Syndrome* and the Harrisburg nuclear accident show the film preceding and predicting the event, making them indistinguishable. Baudrillard proposes "objective irony" as the only critical strategy: turning the system's logic against itself from inside.

## Key quotes

- *"It is precisely when it appears most truthful, most faithful and most in conformity to reality that the image is most diabolical."* (line 169)
- *"Images precede the real to the extent that they invert the causal and logical order of the real and its reproduction."* (line 167)
- *"Coppola made his film the same way the Americans conducted the war… War becomes film, film becomes war, the two united by their mutual overflow of technology."* (lines 202–205)
- *"There are only simulacra, and Harrisburg is a kind of simulation in the second degree."* (line 244)
- *"The strategy of seduction that needs to be invoked in this situation: according to Manichaeism, the reality of the world is a total illusion."* (line 433)

## Why it's in the course

Week 3 pairs Baudrillard with Elkus (*Speedrunning Through The Language-Game*) and Benzel's *New Memory*. Baudrillard provides the theoretical apparatus for understanding how media and computational systems don't reflect reality but *constitute* it — foundational for understanding the W3 seminar's focus on enshittification, AI slop, and consensus reality.

Central to **Apparatus** and **Reversal**. Distrust the apparent innocence of images, AI outputs, recommendation algorithms, simulations.
