## Summary

Diamond surveys agent-based modeling of the Anasazi civilization, where computational simulation recreates population dynamics, settlement patterns, and collapse based on climate data, agricultural rules, and household behavior. The simulation reveals that the Anasazi abandonment of Long House Valley around AD 1275 cannot be fully explained by environmental stress alone — suggesting that cultural, social, or institutional factors beyond the model's parameters drove exodus. Diamond advocates for computational modeling as a tool for understanding complex historical causation.

## Key quotes

- *"Without all those data on rainfall, groundwater, soil types, crop yields and household behaviour, verbal claims such as 'The Anasazi abandoned their homeland because of drought' are as likely to be wrong as right."* (line 55)

## Why it's in the course

Week 10's seminar explicitly names *"Sugarscape, Life with the Artificial Anasazi."* Diamond's piece exemplifies how computational models become instruments for archaeological and historical thinking — blurring the boundary between simulation and reality. ## Relevance to the lab

**Apparatus** and **Loading.** The model's assumptions about household behavior, marriage rules, maize consumption encode modern Western preconceptions. What emerges in simulation may diverge from historical fact, creating an "artificial history" — a case of Loading the Machine that students can study as pedagogical precedent.
