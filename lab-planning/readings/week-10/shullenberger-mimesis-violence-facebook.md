## Summary

Shullenberger traces the influence of René Girard's mimetic theory on Peter Thiel, arguing that Thiel's 2004 investment in Facebook — made shortly after a Stanford symposium on Girard, Strauss, and Schmitt — was not just a financial bet but a political project grounded in Girard's theories of mimetic desire and sacrificial violence. For Girard, desire is always mediated: we want what we see others wanting; this imitation produces rivalries that converge on scapegoats whose sacrifice temporarily purges collective violence. Shullenberger argues that social media platforms are engineered conduits of precisely this mimetic structure — likes, retweets, and trending topics are mimetic signals — and that Thiel, attentive to the fragility of the "founder" as a potential scapegoat, views platforms (and Palantir) as technologies for channeling and containing mimetic violence in an age when the state's sacrificial machinery no longer works. The essay closes by suggesting a counter-reading: Girard's framework can be turned back on Thiel to unmask how platforms administer desire.

## Key quotes

- *"'[Thiel] gave Facebook its first $500,000 investment, he said, because he saw Professor Girard's theories being validated in the concept of social media. "Facebook first spread by word of mouth, and it's about word of mouth, so it's doubly mimetic,"'"* (line 47)
- *"'Man is the creature who does not know what to desire, and who turns to others in order to make up his mind. We desire what others desire because we imitate their desires.'"* (line 61)
- *"The algorithms driving social media, as it turns out, are programmed on mimetic principles."* (line 73)
- *"Thiel invested in and promoted Facebook not simply because Girard's theories led him to foresee the future profitability of the company, but because he saw social media as a mechanism for the containment and channeling of mimetic violence in the face of an ineffectual state."* (line 79)
- *"abuse, harassment, and bullying – the various forms of scapegoating that have become depressing constants of online behavior – are features, not bugs: the platforms' basic social architecture, by concentrating mimetic behavior, also stokes the tendencies toward envy, rivalry, and hatred of the Other that feed online violence."* (line 139)
- *"By revealing that the management of desire confers power, mimetic theory can help us make sense of how platforms administer our desires, and to whose benefit."* (line 141)

## Why it's in the course

This essay names the theory the platforms were built on — not a retrospective critical reading but the operating ideology of at least one principal investor. For art students who will work through and against social media infrastructures, understanding that scapegoating-as-feature is part of the design spec is essential.

## Relevance to the lab

Squarely an **Apparatus** reading: the platform decided, before the user arrived, that desire would be triangulated, rivalries stoked, and scapegoats produced. Also resonates with **Reversal** — Shullenberger models exactly the counter-move the course wants, using the apparatus's own theoretical stack against the purposes of its builders.
