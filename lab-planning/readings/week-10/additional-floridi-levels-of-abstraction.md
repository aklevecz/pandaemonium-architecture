## Summary

Floridi defends epistemological *levelism* — the practice of analyzing systems at multiple levels of abstraction — against recent philosophical critiques. He distinguishes observables, levels of abstraction (LoA), and gradients of abstraction (GoA) with formal precision. Rather than assuming a single "true" level, Floridi shows how different LoAs reveal different properties of the same system. The method applies to everything from wine tasting to chess to biological organization, providing a toolkit for analyzing complex emergent systems without collapsing into reductionism or holism.

## Key quotes

- *"A level of abstraction (LoA) is a finite but non-empty set of observables. No order is assigned to the observables, which are expected to be the building blocks in a theory characterised by their very definition."* (line 127)
- *"The behaviour of a system, at a given LoA, is defined to consist of a predicate whose free variables are observables at that LoA."* (line 145)

## Why it's in the course

Week 10 ("Metaruins") engages abstraction theory directly — the seminar covers "Levelism: epistemological, ontological, methodological, abstraction." Floridi provides formal philosophy for how to move between layers of representation (computational, conceptual, ontological). Grounds Hoffman's Interface Theory and Bostrom's Simulation Hypothesis (same week) in methodological rigor.

## Relevance to the lab

**Apparatus.** Every model encodes an abstraction choice — what's observable, what's hidden. Shift the LoA and create new aesthetic possibilities. Use formalism to denaturalize familiar levels (e.g., analyze "person" at the neurochemical LoA, or "image" at the loss-function LoA). Useful supplementary frame for projects that want to make the apparatus's abstraction layer visible.
