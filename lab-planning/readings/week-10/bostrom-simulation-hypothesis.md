## Summary

Bostrom's 2003 paper presents the simulation argument: given the assumption of substrate-independence (that consciousness can supervene on any suitable computational substrate) and plausible estimates of posthuman computing power, at least one of three propositions must be true — (1) civilizations like ours almost always go extinct before reaching a posthuman stage; (2) posthuman civilizations almost never run ancestor-simulations; or (3) we are almost certainly living in a computer simulation. The argument rests on a "bland indifference principle" that if most minds with our kind of experience are simulated, one should assign a correspondingly high credence to being simulated oneself. Bostrom closes with speculative interpretations: nested simulations, shadow-people, selectively-rendered microphysics, a "naturalistic theogony" in which simulators function as gods with power over their simulants, and a surprisingly mundane practical upshot ("the implications are not all that radical").

## Key quotes

- *"This paper argues that *at least one* of the following propositions is true: (1) the human species is very likely to go extinct before reaching a 'posthuman' stage; (2) any posthuman civilization is extremely unlikely to run a significant number of simulations of their evolutionary history (or variations thereof); (3) we are almost certainly living in a computer simulation."* (line 7)
- *"in order to get a realistic simulation of human experience, much less is needed – only whatever is required to ensure that the simulated humans, interacting in normal human ways with their simulated environment, don't notice any irregularities."* (line 59)
- *"Should any error occur, the director could easily edit the states of any brains that have become aware of an anomaly before it spoils the simulation."* (line 61)
- *"While the world we see is in some sense 'real', it is not located at the fundamental level of reality."* (line 155)
- *"the posthumans running a simulation are like gods in relation to the people inhabiting the simulation: the posthumans created the world we see; they are of superior intelligence; they are 'omnipotent' in the sense that they can interfere in the workings of our world even in ways that violate its physical laws."* (line 161)

## Why it's in the course

The simulation hypothesis is the secular-theology backdrop for a lot of Silicon Valley's self-image and for AI-safety/longtermist discourse students will encounter. Reading it directly — rather than through memes — lets students see the specific metaphysical moves (substrate-independence, indifference principle, fidelity-by-patching) that the culture of AI absorbs.

## Relevance to the lab

Primarily **Apparatus** stance, as counterpoint to Hoffman: if Hoffman says the interface is selected by evolution, Bostrom asks what happens if the interface is authored by designers who can edit belief-states when anomalies appear. Also useful for **Loading the machine** — the "patch the microphysics on observation" image is a direct analogue to how generative models fill in detail only when looked at.
