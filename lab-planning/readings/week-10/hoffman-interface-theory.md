## Summary

Hoffman argues, on evolutionary grounds, that perception does not approximate objective reality — it is a species-specific user interface, like the icons on a desktop, tuned for fitness rather than truth. Using examples (jewel beetles that mistake beer bottles for mates, herring gull chicks, *Portia* jumping spiders), and a formal "interface game" simulation, he shows that natural selection systematically drives veridical perception to extinction in favor of cheap, fast, satisficing categories. The conventional Bayesian-reconstruction view of perception is trapped in "Bayes' Circle" — using posteriors to validate the priors that generated them. Hoffman distinguishes taking icons *seriously* (not petting the tiger) from taking them *literally* (thinking the tiger-as-perceived is the thing itself), and closes by proposing that space, time, and physical objects may themselves be interface constructs.

## Key quotes

- *"our perceptions constitute a species-specific user interface that guides behavior in a niche. Just as the icons of a PC's interface hide the complexity of the computer, so our perceptions usefully hide the complexity of the world, and guide adaptive behavior."* (line 9)
- *"The error in this argument is fundamental: Natural selection optimizes fitness, not veridicality. The two are distinct and, indeed, can be at odds."* (line 51)
- *"I don't pet wild tigers for the same reason I don't carelessly drag a file icon to the trash bin. I don't take the icon literally, as though it resembles the real file. But I do take it seriously."* (line 129)
- *"Perceptual categories are endogenous constructs of a subjective Umwelt, not exogenous mirrors of an objective world."* (line 121)
- *"natural selection drives true perception to swift extinction: Nowhere in evolution, even among the most complex of organisms, will you find that natural selection drives truth to fixation… Natural selection rewards fecundity, not factuality, so it shapes interfaces, not telescopes on truth."* (line 222)

## Why it's in the course

Hoffman gives the seminar a precise vocabulary for the constitutive, non-neutral character of any perceptual apparatus — biological, cultural, or computational. It reframes "seeing what the model sees" from a technical problem to an ontological one: every observer, silicon or carbon, is running an interface selected for fitness in a niche.

## Relevance to the lab

Directly feeds the **Apparatus** stance: an ML system's outputs are not a window onto reality but a species-specific interface selected by a training regime. Also informs **Creative Act** — the spectator is not recovering a ground truth but completing the work through their own interface.
