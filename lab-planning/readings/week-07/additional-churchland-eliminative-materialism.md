## Summary

Churchland argues that folk psychology — our common-sense theory of mental states, beliefs, desires, and propositional attitudes — is a radically false empirical theory that will eventually be *displaced* by neuroscience, not reduced to it. Rather than protecting ordinary psychology from scientific critique, recognizing it as theory makes it subject to empirical criticism like any other theory. Churchland examines explanatory failures, historical stagnation, and incoherence with physical science to mount a case for elimination. Addresses functionalist defenses and sketches speculative scenarios of how human cognitive life might be reconstituted without propositional attitudes.

## Key quotes

- *"Eliminative materialism is the thesis that our commonsense conception of psychological phenomena constitutes a radically false theory, a theory so fundamentally defective that both the principles and the ontology of that theory will eventually be displaced, rather than smoothly reduced, by completed neuroscience."* (line 33)
- *"Folk psychology suffers explanatory failures on an epic scale, that it has been stagnant for at least twenty-five centuries, and that its categories appear (so far) to be incommensurable with or orthogonal to the categories of the background physical science."* (line 122)
- *"The real laws governing our internal activities are defined over different and much more complex kinematical states and configurations, as are the normative criteria for developmental integrity and intellectual virtue."* (line 220)

## Why it's in the course

Week 7 (*Eliminative Materialism and Neurophilosophy*) is explicitly titled after Churchland. The syllabus cites "the work of the Churchlands, Sellars, Chalmers, et al" as foundational. Churchland grounds the course's engagement with how AI and neuroscience challenge traditional categories of human mind and agency.

## Relevance to the lab

**Apparatus.** Churchland names what folk psychology "decided" before we arrived (that mind = propositional attitudes). This reframing invites students to interrogate whether the tools they use for generative AI or pattern recognition encode similar hidden assumptions about cognition that future science will render obsolete.
