## Summary

Negarestani opens *Intelligence and Spirit* (2018) with a Hegelian-functionalist thesis: mind is not a thing or a substance but is *only what it does*, and what it does is constituted by the social, public, language-bearing activity of agents recognizing one another. The chapter uses the program of artificial general intelligence as a thought experiment — a "photographic negative" — for clarifying what we (humans) are by clarifying what we are *determinately* not. Intelligence is the labor of rendering the world intelligible from the standpoint of an "other" reality that resists; this is what makes geist a project of freedom rather than a description of an interior self.

## Key quotes

- *"This book argues, from a functionalist perspective, that mind is only what it does; and that what it does is first and foremost realized by the sociality of agents, which itself is primarily and ontologically constituted by the semantic space of a public language."* (line 5)
- *"Our main objective is not to investigate the possibility of constructing an artificial general intelligence or to review the popular narratives of posthuman superintelligence, but to think about AGI and, even more generally, computers, as an outside view of ourselves. This is an objective labour, so to speak, whereby AGI or computers tell us what we are in virtue of what we are determinately not"* (line 31)
- *"Mind is not a thing: it is only what it does."* (line 79)
- *"Intelligence only turns into intelligence when it loses its passivity, when it actively begins to render reality intelligible and, in so doing, begins to re-engineer the reality of itself."* (line 223)
- *"Personhood is the product of the impersonality of reason, and consciousness of the individuated self is an artefact of an individuating recognitive space in which all selves are incorporated. In short, there is no consciousness without self-consciousness."* (line 275)

## Why it's in the course

Negarestani offers the seminar's most rigorous statement of the anti-anthropomorphic, social-functional view of mind that the syllabus uses to push past both "machines can think!" hype and "only humans can really understand" defensiveness. He gives students the conceptual tools to take AGI seriously as a thought experiment without conceding the metaphysics of consumer-AI marketing.

## Relevance to the lab

Apparatus: Negarestani's insistence that mind is constituted in a *public* semantic space gives a way to read every model as already-social — the apparatus is a deprivatized space others built before you. Creative Act: his picture of intelligence as labor that requires an "other" who pushes back maps surprisingly well onto Duchamp's two-pole loop in which the spectator completes the work — both insist on reciprocity rather than monadic genius.
