## Summary

Ethnographic introduction tracing machine gambling's rise from marginal to dominant in Las Vegas and its expansion nationwide. Rather than a story of individual pathology or moral failing, Schüll frames addiction as a human–machine relationship: the specific affordances of slot machine design (rapid event frequency, continuous play, persistent sensory feedback, visual/auditory reward signals) interact with players' desires for a dissociative "machine zone" where time, money, and identity dissolve. The text rejects both determinism ("machines cause addiction") and voluntarism ("only weak-willed people get addicted"), instead examining the *coproduction* of addiction through design intentions, player vulnerabilities, regulatory ecologies, and the affective landscape of contemporary capitalism. "Time-on-device" (TOD) is the industry's own metric.

- *"The potential for addiction emerges when repeated interaction with a specific object or array of objects (a drug, a game of chance, a computer) reliably produces a desirable subjective shift."* (line 211)
- *"The account of addiction to gambling machines that I present here does not seek to locate the ultimate cause of addiction discretely within gamblers or gambling machines but rather in the dynamic interaction between the two."* (line 243)
