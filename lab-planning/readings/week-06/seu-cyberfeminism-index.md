## Summary

Seu's introduction positions the *Cyberfeminism Index* as a living archive of three decades of artistic, activist, and theoretical interventions into technology and gender. Cyberfeminism is defined not as a singular manifesto or movement but as a "rhizomatic" network: "anti-genealogical, anti-authorial," always mutating. The collection gathers YACK (theory, bibliography) and HACK (practice, DIY, direct action) across global contexts — from VNS Matrix (1991) through contemporary xenofeminism, Afrofuturism, and digital rights work. Seu emphasizes *gathering* over *hunting*, centering the methodological practice of the index itself as collective, non-hierarchical accumulation.

## Key quotes

- *"Cyberfeminism is a mutating word with a nebulous history. Its evolution is less a single root system with multiple branches than a network of entangled rhizomes, constantly and multidirectionally moving."* (line 29)
- *"By now, three decades after its origination, cyberfeminism has shifted from a loose artistic movement exploring the emancipatory potential of cyberspace toward a collective drive to provide software, hardware, and wetware education and to get marginalized groups online."* (line 31)
- *"Gathering, for Le Guin, is not a masculine, techno-utopian process of disruption or of moving fast and breaking things, but the methodical, deep labor that comes from 'looking around, rather than looking ahead,' from gathering rather than hunting."* (line 33)
- *"Its strength and resilience lies in its ability to morph and change through the actions of those who identify as cyberfeminists, and also of those who challenge its purpose and validity."* (line 11)

## Why it's in the course

The Index anchors Week 6's investigation of cyberfeminism as both historical movement and ongoing practice. Seu's curatorial methodology — gathering without canonizing, including critique and contradiction — models how to engage with technology and gender not as settled questions but as sites of perpetual contestation and reconfiguration. Pairs with Quicho and Hadnagy.

## Relevance to the lab

**Creative Act.** Seu's emphasis on gathering, citation, and collective knowledge-making resonates with Duchamp's two-pole feedback between maker and spectator. The Index itself is a creative work whose completion depends on readers' participation and contribution. The curatorial practice demonstrates how artistic labor can refuse the logic of individual authorship while remaining rigorous and accountable — a possible mode for student projects that resist the solo-genius frame AI tools tend to reinforce.
