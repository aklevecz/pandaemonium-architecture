## Summary

Hadnagy defines social engineering as any act that influences a person to take an action that may not be in their best interest, emphasizing that the term is not inherently negative — the same psychological principles used for "attack" enable persuasion, trust-building, and rapport. He outlines the "SE Pyramid": Open Source Intelligence (OSINT), Pretext Development, Attack Plan, Attack Launch, Reporting. The text reveals how attackers exploit cognitive biases, emotional triggers, and the gap between humans' "alpha mode" (alert, observant) and "beta mode" (daydreaming, susceptible). Key mechanisms include oxytocin (trust response), dopamine (reward), and the triggering of emotions that bypass rational deliberation.

## Key quotes

- *"Social engineering takes the way humans are wired to make decisions and exploits the vulnerabilities in those processes. The goal of the social engineer is to get you to make a decision without thinking."* (line 63)
- *"The more emotions are triggered, the less you think rationally. The less you think rationally, the quicker you will decide based solely on the emotions triggered."* (line 82)
- *"Your brain releases oxytocin not just when you trust someone, but also when you feel that someone else has given you trust. According to Dr. Zak's research, this phenomenon has been demonstrated in person, over the phone, over the Internet, and even when you can't see the person who is doing the 'trusting.'"* (line 134)
- *"Becoming a critical thinker is what allowed me to learn how to truly understand social engineering."* (intro)

## Why it's in the course

Week 6 examines how technology mediates gender, desire, and vulnerability. Hadnagy's framework reveals the apparatus by which systems (human, digital, organizational) can be exploited or redirected. Presented not to teach attack but to reveal the decision-making architectures that both users and designers reckon with — and to ask whether art can use these same mechanisms toward different ends.

## Relevance to the lab

**Apparatus** and **Reversal.** Hadnagy maps the decision trees and emotional levers that systems (and designers) exploit. Understanding social engineering teaches students how to identify and invert the apparatus. An artist working with these principles might create interventions that expose or scramble the normal operation of persuasion — making visible what designers typically hide.
