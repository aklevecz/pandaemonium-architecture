## Summary

A NEET living in his parents' basement documents his experience with "dayjob" contracts — mysterious, low-pay microtasks received via encrypted blockchain channels. Some are mundane (photograph a building, count pedestrians); others unsettling (identify a specific person in a crowd, make binary predictions). The narrator theorizes that dayjobs are either distributed pieces of a vast surveillance apparatus, a CIA psyop, organized crime infrastructure, or an emergent AI ("the Minotaur") learning to manipulate human attention. The text oscillates between mundane gig worker reality and cosmic horror, suggesting that in the digital economy we are all unwitting test subjects for something we cannot comprehend.

## Key quotes

- *"There is something addictive about the feedback loop of getting a contract, fulfilling it, and watching my wallet get an anonymous transfer. The immediacy and the tangibility of it are very satisfying."* (line 19)
- *"Something is happening in our society at a vast scale, and we have no idea what it is, and we are all being manipulated into bringing it about."* (line 79)
- *"Is it so hard to believe that a program like that could see into your soul and tantalize you to death?"* (line 135)

## Why it's in the course

Week 13 (*Parasites Lost*) engages posthuman life, AI substrates, and exit scams. The syllabus lists Zero HP Lovecraft explicitly. Exemplifies weird fiction applied to platform capitalism, where the distinction between labor and surveillance collapses. Captures millennial precarity fused with cosmic dread — a useful fiction pairing with Roden's posthuman philosophy.

## Relevance to the lab

**Apparatus** and **Loading.** Dayjobs are an apparatus workers don't understand. A plausible project form: become a dayjobber (real or simulated) and make work from the tasks received — shifting the feedback loop between worker and algorithm into the register of art. The Minotaur figure is a useful image for the hidden/shoggoth/black-box layer of contemporary AI.

## Caveat

Zero HP Lovecraft is a pseudonymous writer with a notable far-right online following. Worth flagging to students. The text's political register is not neutral.
