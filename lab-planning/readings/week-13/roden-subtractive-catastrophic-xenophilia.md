## Summary

Roden uses Badiou's method of subtraction (thinking what cannot be thought; exhibiting gaps in representation) to expose a paradox in posthumanist theory: posthumanism claims to think the posthuman, yet the posthuman — by definition — exceeds all human categories. This produces *xenophilia*: a groundless compulsion to become what we cannot yet think. Roden argues posthumanism is not an ethics but a *counter-ethics* that recapitulates the effects of our "posthuman predicament" (technoscientific transformation of life). Resistance means creating zones of opacity, refusing both the sapience filter (rationality = agency) and the sentience filter (feeling = agency).

## Key quotes

- *"It is thought only in terms of traces that imply its subtraction or unobjectifiability. Subtraction consequently allows us to think the possibility of an 'Outside' that cannot be represented in discourse."* (line 29)
- *"Insofar as Xenophilia is satisfied it cannot be. Insofar as Xenophilia is, it cannot be satisfied."* (line 221)
- *"Subtraction xeno-thinks the posthuman by removing the normative filters that, however fragile, allowed us to keep the Outside at a philosophical remove."* (line 185)

Trying to "solve" AI through better representation or ethics is trapped in the apparatus's logic.
