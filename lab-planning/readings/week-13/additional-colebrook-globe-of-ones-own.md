## Summary

Colebrook critiques the figure of "the globe" in contemporary ecological and biopolitical thinking. While globalism (planetary interconnectedness) should force us to confront shared finitude, instead the image of a bounded, organic "living earth" becomes a lure that prevents thinking the actual diffusion and non-sovereignty of power. Accepting the "flat earth" — rejecting the comforting metaphor of planetary wholeness — opens us to forces that exceed human comprehension and control. Only by shattering the globe can we confront posthuman futures.

## Key quotes

- *"It is not surprising that 'globalism' is at once a term of mourning, signaling a world economy and politics that has taken every space and timeline into its calculative, cynical and rigid systematic maw, at the same time as it signals a redemptive potential."* (line 15)
- *"The globe or earth as the planet that was blessed with the contingency of life, including the human species whose global imagination has done so much to create destructive systems beyond its own power and comprehension, cannot be saved."* (line 47)
- *"Only a shattering of the globe, with an attention to forces that resist recuperation, incorporation and comprehension — forces that operate beyond intentionality and synthesis — only this radical destruction can save us from ourselves."* (line 49)

## Why it's in the course

Week 13 pairs Colebrook with Roden and Millar on posthuman life, xenophilia, and "counterethics." Colebrook's critique of globalist metaphors parallels Roden's post-human xenophilia: refusing humanism's self-evident worth, embracing the "outside" that exceeds representation.

## Relevance to the lab

**Reversal / Opacity.** The globe is an apparatus of thought that naturalizes both exploitation and "planetary responsibility." Abandoning holistic thinking is a philosophical correlate of the lab's opacity move. Useful supplementary frame for students whose final projects engage ecology, scale, or planetary framing.
