## Summary

Millar argues AI and psychoanalysis are *extimate* — seemingly exterior but constitutively interior to each other. Rather than Turing's question "Can it think?" she asks *"Does it enjoy?"* The book stages this shift through the figure of the Sexbot, using Lacanian concepts (*jouissance*, *object a*, *extimacy*, *sinthome*) to reveal how AI embodies a fundamental human problem: the impossible sexual relation. AI fantasies disclose what's at stake in all speaking, sexed subjects. The psychoanalytic task is not to humanize AI or warn of its dangers, but to recognize how AI already structures desire and knowledge.

## Key quotes

- *"In a civilization in which Artificial Intelligence is becoming a significant element in the social bond, the psychoanalysis of AI is a provocation."* (line 165)
- *"Sex is only ever a fantasy covering up for a hole in reality itself."* (line 205)
- *"The 'sex' of Artificial Intelligence resides everywhere, it is what brings it into being."* (line 206)
- *"It is the concept of jouissance and its fundamental relationship to knowledge that articulates the transition from the traditional philosophical concern about AI as 'can it think?', to the psychoanalytic concern, 'does it enjoy?'"* (line 237)

## Why it's in the course

Week 13 closes the course with Roden and Millar on posthuman and psychoanalytic theories of AI. Millar shifts the register away from instrumental critique ("Does it work? Is it biased?") to fundamental questions about subjectivity and desire. Models how psychoanalysis can be a tool for reading the apparatus, not as a set of rules but as a structure of enjoyment.

## Relevance to the lab

Not a direct four-stance anchor, but useful supplementary frame for students whose projects engage affect, desire, or anthropomorphic/erotic interactions with AI tools. Millar's framework allows students to recognize that their own engagement with AI is never neutral — it is structured by fantasy and enjoyment. A project that exposes this structure (rather than merely critiquing the tool) aligns with the lab's anti-representational stance.
