## Summary

Brusseau (Theoria, 2020) updates Deleuze's 1990 *Postscript on the Societies of Control* with thirty years of data-broker, predictive-analytics, and platform reality. His central thesis: today's control society "coerces without prohibitions, and through incentives that are not grim but enjoyable, even euphoric, because they compel individuals to obey their own personal information." Where Deleuze's e-card might be "rejected on a given day," today's apparatus does not block — it lures, suggests, recommends. Brusseau preserves Deleuze's triptych structure (historical / logical / programmatic) and concludes by sketching two extreme post-privacy strategies: *transparency* (proactively over-share so platforms serve you better) and *discontinuity* (deliberately become someone else so the accumulated profile misfires).

## Key quotes

- *"The central claim is that today's advancing control society coerces without prohibitions, and through incentives that are not grim but enjoyable, even euphoric because they compel individuals to obey their own personal information."* (line 7)
- *"What Deleuze perceived as a world of oppression, has become a reality of competition."* (line 71)
- *"Tracking and predicting give way to targeting (Walters and Bekker 2017). Targeting means offering incentives to specific individuals or delimited groups that have been tailored to appeal to their unique tastes, anxieties, and yearnings. When it works, people who were tracked and predicted become directed."* (line 109)
- *"The strategic requirement for control today is no explicit prohibitions, no blocked possibilities, no forbidden ways. There are only opportunities and temptations. Some lead you towards, others lead you away. All are nearly impossible to resist or escape. What can be resisted when nothing is being forbidden or denied? Where can escape lead when every direction converts into a trajectory of incentives?"* (lines 119–121)
- *"With some counterfeit personal information and creative swiping, the algorithms designed to locate data-verified compatible matches can be perverted to generate encounters entirely detached from the familiar rules of attraction... The platforms gathering information and spreading coercion also enable regenerated data about tastes, values, vulnerabilities, and desires that crack identity profiles, that crash algorithms, and that lose control."* (lines 255–261)

## Why it's in the course

Brusseau is the most readable contemporary update of Deleuze for a class that needs students to grasp how surveillance has changed register from oppression to "joys of marketing." His framing of the control society as enjoyable rather than grim is essential context for any week on recommendation, prediction, or persuasive design — and his closing taxonomy (transparency, obfuscation per Brunton & Nissenbaum, discontinuity) maps directly onto the syllabus's reversal/zones-of-opacity stance.

## Relevance to the lab

Apparatus: Brusseau gives the cleanest contemporary statement of "control without prohibition" — the apparatus's prior decisions are now phrased as opportunities to lure rather than walls to block. Reversal: the discontinuity strategy ("becoming-other," counterfeit swiping, life-jarring opportunities) is a direct lab prompt for using a predictive system against its own profile of you.
