## Summary

Malabou's introduction makes two yoked moves. First, she argues — citing Changeux and the post-1979 neurosciences — that the brain is *plastic* in the strict sense: it both receives and gives form, and on a third register (plastique, the explosive) it can annihilate form. Plasticity is the constitutive historicity of the brain, and "humans make their own brain, but they do not know that they make it." Second, she warns that the dominant culture has substituted *flexibility* for plasticity: a docile capacity to take the fold dictated by neoliberal labor ("part-time jobs, temporary contracts, the demand for absolute mobility and adaptability"), described in vocabulary borrowed from neuroscience (networks, connections, modulation) and naturalized via Boltanski and Chiapello's "new spirit of capitalism." The book's project is to liberate plasticity from flexibility — to insist on the brain's capacity to *give* form and even to refuse form, not just to receive it.

## Key quotes

- *"The brain is a work, and we do not know it. We are its subjects—authors and products at once—and we do not know it."* (line 5)
- *"Talking about the plasticity of the brain thus amounts to thinking of the brain as something modifiable, 'formable,' and formative at the same time."* (line 29)
- *"But it must be remarked that plasticity is also the capacity to annihilate the very form it is able to receive or create. We should not forget that plastique, from which we get the words plastiquage and plastiquer, is an explosive substance made of nitroglycerine and nitrocellulose, capable of causing violent explosions."* (line 31)
- *"The guiding question of the present effort should thus be formulated: What should we do so that consciousness of the brain does not purely and simply coincide with the spirit of capitalism? We will formulate the following thesis: today, the true sense of plasticity is hidden, and we tend constantly to substitute for it its mistaken cognate, flexibility."* (line 63)
- *"Flexibility is plasticity minus its genius."* (line 63)

## Why it's in the course

Malabou supplies a key term — *plasticity vs. flexibility* — for the seminar's interrogation of cybernetic adaptability and "neuroplasticity"-style self-improvement discourse. She insists that the brain's analogy to a network is not just empirical fact but ideological framing, which is exactly the move the syllabus wants students to be able to perform on AI systems too.

## Relevance to the lab

Apparatus: Malabou's central claim — that "neuronal functioning and social functioning interdetermine each other" through a naturalization effect — is a model exercise of naming what the apparatus already decided about the user (in this case, the user's brain). Reversal: her *plastique* register (annihilation of form, refusal to take the fold) is a productive frame for any lab assignment that resists the model's smoothing/flexibilizing default.
