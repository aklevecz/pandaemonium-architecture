## Summary

Eugene Wei's 2019 essay (originally a long blog post on *Remains of the Day*) proposes that social networks should be analyzed not only as utility products with network effects but as systems that issue and manage "social capital" — a kind of currency earned through proof-of-work rituals (a witty tweet, an interesting square photo, a six-second Vine, a lip-synch). Wei frames this in explicit analogy to ICOs/cryptocurrencies: each network mints a token, scarcity is engineered through performance hurdles, and early adopters accumulate compounding follower counts. The essay covers Facebook's harvard.edu credential as one of "the most powerful slingshots of elitism," Twitter's transition from dull life-updates to "late-stage performative Twitter" (driven by Favstar/Favrd leaderboards), Snapchat's Best Friends list, and "evaporative cooling" — when the wrong demographic shows up and the high-status users flee.

## Key quotes

- *"People are status-seeking monkeys / People seek out the most efficient path to maximizing social capital"* (lines 27–29)
- *"Each new social network issues a new form of social capital, a token. / You must show proof of work to earn the token. / Over time it becomes harder and harder to mine new tokens on each social network, creating built-in scarcity."* (lines 121–125)
- *"Almost every social network of note had an early signature proof of work hurdle. For Facebook it was posting some witty text-based status update. For Instagram, it was posting an interesting square photo. For Vine, an entertaining 6-second video. For Twitter, it was writing an amusing bit of text of 140 characters or fewer."* (line 153)
- *"Facebook launched with one of the most famous proof of work hurdles in the world: you had to be a student at Harvard. By requiring a harvard.edu email address, Facebook drafted off of one of the most elite cultural filters in the world. It's hard to think of many more powerful slingshots of elitism."* (line 235)
- *"Read Twitter today and hardly any of the tweets are the mundane life updates of its awkward pre-puberty years. We are now in late-stage performative Twitter, where nearly every tweet is hungry as hell for favorites and retweets, and everyone is a trained pundit or comedian."* (line 205)

## Why it's in the course

Wei provides the operator's-eye view of social platforms as engineered status games — written by an investor/insider rather than a critic. Pairs naturally with Brusseau (control through incentives), Fogg (captology), and any week on attention economics, because it makes the platform-design intent explicit and admiring rather than indignant.

## Relevance to the lab

Apparatus: Wei is naming what platforms decided about the user before the user arrived — the proof-of-work hurdle, the leaderboards, the scarcity engineering. Reversal: his framing is also instruction-manual-grade material for a lab that wants to design *anti*-status networks, or to deliberately misuse status mechanics for non-extractive ends.
