## Summary

Good (the Bletchley Park cryptanalyst who worked with Turing) writes the paper every AI-risk discourse for the next sixty years builds on. He defines an "ultraintelligent machine" as one that surpasses every intellectual activity of any human, notes that since machine design is such an activity the first such machine could design better machines, and predicts an "intelligence explosion" — making this the *last invention man need ever make* (provided it is "docile enough to tell us how to keep it under control"). The bulk of the paper is a technical proposal: ultraparallel artificial neural nets (possibly with microminiature radio transmitters for connectivity), probabilistic "regeneration" as a theory of meaning, Hebb-modified "subassembly" cell-assembly theory, Bayesian recall, and reinforcement learning with stochastic synaptic updates. It is, quite literally, a 1965 sketch of deep learning.

## Key quotes

- *"The survival of man depends on the early construction of an ultraintelligent machine."* (line 23)
- *"Let an ultraintelligent machine be defined as a machine that can far surpass all the intellectual activities of any man however clever. Since the design of machines is one of these intellectual activities, an ultraintelligent machine could design even better machines; there would then unquestionably be an 'intelligence explosion,' and the intelligence of man would be left far behind. Thus the first ultraintelligent machine is the last invention that man need ever make, provided that the machine is docile enough to tell us how to keep it under control."* (line 51)
- *"It is curious that this point is made so seldom outside of science fiction. It is sometimes worthwhile to take science fiction seriously."* (line 51)
- *"The machines will create social problems, but they might also be able to solve them in addition to those that have been created by microbes and men. Such machines will be feared and respected, and perhaps even loved."* (line 61)
- *"It is more probable than not that, within the twentieth century, an ultraintelligent machine will be built and that it will be the last invention that man need make, since it will lead to an 'intelligence explosion.' This will transform society in an unimaginable way."* (line 633)
- *"Probably Man will construct the deus ex machina in his own image."* (line 33)

## Why it's in the course

Week 4's *Pervasive Global Cognitive Automaton* segment (Stross's "very slow AIs," the open letter on AI moratorium, Dick's post-biological evolution, Turing's *Computing Machinery and Intelligence*) is anchored by Good's paper as the primary-source origin of the "intelligence explosion" / Singularity frame. It is also the hinge between Week 1's TESCREAL diagnosis (Gebru/Torres trace the AGI race back through exactly this lineage, and Kurzweil/Yudkowsky explicitly cite Good) and the Week 4 lab unit on Copilot/Cursor/encapsulation.

## Relevance to the lab

**Apparatus (i).** Good's text is the genealogical receipt that many of the "alignment," "AI safety," and "recursive self-improvement" framings students will encounter in 2025 media and tooling are continuations of a 1965 speculative monograph, not neutral technical discoveries. Naming Good's paper as the ideological source of the intelligence-explosion frame is a clean stance-(i) move — particularly the *"docile enough to tell us how to keep it under control"* qualifier, which exposes the unexamined assumption of the entire "aligned AGI" discourse.
