## Summary

Platforms are a third institutional form alongside states and markets: they "set the stage for actions to unfold through ordered emergence" rather than premeditated master planning. A platform standardizes its *means* (rigid protocols, fixed standards) while remaining agnostic about *ends* (user-generated outcomes). The Stack — Bratton's megastructure — is a vertically integrated platform of platforms: geologic layers, fiber networks, computational layers, and human-inhabited zones, all operating through standardized protocols. Platforms govern through standardization and generative entrenchment (switching costs rise once users invest). They are formally neutral but uniquely ideological: each platform's coordination strategy generates different politics. Political power is no longer solely a matter of law or economics — it is baked into protocols, architectures, and standards.

## Why it's in the course

Week 4's seminar covers planetary computation, Charles Stross's "very slow AIs," and pernicious feedback loops in Trapwire/Predpol/Palantir. Bratton supplies the systems-level theory of how platforms function as political machines. This reading synthesizes earlier weeks' focus on images, antimemetics, and apparatus by showing that the architecture you choose (stack layers, protocols, standards) determines what kinds of relationships, data flows, and human behaviors become possible.

## Relevance to the lab

Primarily **Apparatus** and **Loading the machine**. If artistic choice is what you feed the apparatus, Bratton teaches that the apparatus's shape — its stack, its protocols — already determines what can be fed and what can emerge. Also informs **Reversal**: understanding stack architecture lets you identify *where* zones of opacity are, which layers you can corrupt or refuse, how to exploit gaps between layers where standardization breaks down.
