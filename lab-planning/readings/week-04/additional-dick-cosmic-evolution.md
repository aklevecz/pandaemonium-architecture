## Summary

Dick, then NASA's Chief Historian, narrates the 20th-century consolidation of "cosmic evolution" — the notion that physical, biological, and cultural evolution form a single continuous story from the Big Bang onward — and shows how it became NASA's organizing research program through exobiology, SETI, the Drake Equation, and later the Astrobiology Institute. In the chapter's payoff, Dick argues that cosmic evolution permits three endpoint scenarios: the *physical* universe (humanity alone), the *biological* universe (SETI's premise), or — his own contribution — a *postbiological universe* in which cultural evolution has long since replaced biological intelligences with artificial ones. On Stapledonian time scales, and given any non-trivial value of L, this may be the default outcome: "some predict Earth will be postbiological in a few generations."

## Key quotes

- *"Cultural evolution in a biological universe may have already produced, or replaced, biologicals with artificial intelligence, constituting what I have called a 'postbiological universe.'"* (line 255)
- *"If intelligence is highly valued for its evolutionary advantage, extraterrestrials would long ago have sought the best way to improve their intelligence, and it is likely to have involved artificial intelligence, yielding the postbiological universe… Indeed, some predict Earth will be postbiological in a few generations."* (line 261)
- *"The adjustment will be to the biological, or even postbiological, universe, in which intelligences are likely to be superior to us."* (line 183)
- *"Such a postbiological universe would have sweeping implications for SETI strategies, for our worldview, and for the destiny of life on Earth if it has already happened throughout the universe. We may see our own future in the evolution of extraterrestrial civilizations, perhaps another motivation for searching."* (line 263)
- *"Whatever its long-term destiny, it is surely the destiny of humanity in the near future to follow the trail of scientific evidence wherever it may lead even if it means abandoning old scientific, philosophical, and theological ideas."* (line 275)

## Relevance to the lab

**Apparatus (i).** Dick's text demonstrates how a piece of speculative cosmological narrative ("postbiological universe") moves from SETI-era SF into NASA policy language and back into Silicon Valley longtermism. For the lab, it is the primary source that lets students name one of the deepest apparatus-level narratives baked into contemporary AI discourse: the framing of machine intelligence as the *next cosmic step*. That framing is not a neutral background — it is a policy artifact, and Dick's chapter (published by NASA) is where you can see it become official.
