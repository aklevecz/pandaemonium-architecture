## Tier A — direct lab-frame material

### Karen Barad, *Agential Realism* (Week 5)

File: `Karen Barad, Agential Realism from Meeting the universe halfway…md`.

**Why it matters for the lab:** gives the lab its core word — *apparatus* —
and the argument that there are no neutral tools, only boundary-making
practices.

- *"Apparatuses are not passive observing instruments; on the contrary,
  they are productive of (and part of) phenomena"* (p. 146).
- *"Apparatuses are specific material-discursive practices (they are not
  merely laboratory setups that embody human concepts and take
  measurements); … apparatuses produce differences that matter — they are
  boundary-making practices that are formative of matter and meaning"* (p. 148).
- *"Relations do not follow relata, but the other way around"* (p. 136–137).
- *"Agency is not an attribute but the ongoing reconfigurings of the
  world"* (p. 141).

**For critique:** the student's apparatus is not a camera (a camera shows
you what's there). It's an apparatus in Barad's sense: a specific
material-discursive arrangement that *enacts cuts* on the world before the
user gets to act. The lab's first move is naming those cuts.

### Benzel, *Notes on À bruit secret* (Week 1 reading, own writing)

File: `Notes on À bruit secret_ Notes on Duchamp, Cybernetics, CIA, and Organized Crime.md`.

**Why it matters:** establishes Duchamp's *Creative Act* (1957) as the
course's theory of art-making. Art is a two-pole feedback loop; the
spectator completes the work.

- Duchamp (1957), in Benzel's note: *"Let us consider two important
  factors, the two poles of the creation of art: the artist on the one
  hand, and on the other the spectator who later becomes the posterity."*
- Duchamp: *"This 'art coefficient' is a personal expression of art 'à
  l'état brut,' that is, still in raw state which must be 'refined' … by
  the spectator … the role of the spectator is to determine the weight of
  the work on the aesthetic scale."*
- Jacques, quoted in note: *"Duchamp's desire to exchange with the
  spectator is today the foundation of any and every human-computer
  interaction."*

**For critique:** the "first spectator" milestone (Week 11) operationalizes
this. Every project has to pass through a spectator before it exists as
art.

### Benzel, *Notes on Thinking Machine Imaginaries* (Week 2 reading, own writing)

File: `Notes on Thinking Machine Imaginaries_ Llull, Leibniz, and Lovelace to 'Shoggoth with a Smiley Face'.md`.

**Why it matters:** gives the loading move. Llull's wheels are the same in
1305 and today; what changed is what gets fed in.

- Borges on Llull, quoted in note: *"We would load the machine
  differently, no doubt with the words Entropy, Time, Electrons … Or with
  Surplus Value, Proletariat, Capitalism, Class Struggle …"*
- Benzel's gloss: *"The various systems, imaginaries, their analogical
  'fit', the complexity of the structures themselves and their internal
  interrelations and recombinations, are what matters."*
- Lovelace: *"It might act upon other things besides number, were objects
  found whose mutual relations could be expressed by those of the abstract
  science of operations."*

**For critique:** the student's artistic decision is the corpus, not the
prompt. Turning in the corpus alongside the output is the evidence of
craft.

### Tiqqun, *The Cybernetic Hypothesis* (introductory reading)

File: `Tiqqun - The Cybernetic Hypothesis (2001).md`.

**Why it matters:** gives the *opacity* move — art as zone of offensive
opacity, as fog, as black bloc within the cybernetic matrix.

- *"Establishing a zone of opacity where people can circulate and
  experiment freely without bringing in the Empire's information flows,
  means producing 'anonymous singularities,' recreating the conditions for
  a possible experience, an experience which will not be immediately
  flattened out by a binary machine assigning a meaning/direction to it."*
  (line 603)
- *"The important thing is maybe to create vacuoles of non-communication,
  interrupters who escape control. Yes, the important thing for us is to
  have opacity zones, opening cavities, empty intervals, black blocs
  within the cybernetic matrix of power."* (line 607)
- *"Fog is a vital response to the imperative of clarity, transparency,
  which is the first imprint of imperial power on bodies."* (line 599)

**For critique:** "clarity" is not a neutral virtue. Work that refuses to
be legible to the feedback loop is doing political work.

### Brunton & Nissenbaum, *Obfuscation* (Week 11)

File: `Finn Brunton, Helen Nissenbaum, from Obfuscation, a users guide for privacy and protest.md`.

**Why it matters:** by far the most usable text in the course for an
art-school lab. Gives a vocabulary of reversal tactics that are *already*
partly framed as art. Intro + core cases 1.1–1.8.

- *"Obfuscation is the deliberate addition of ambiguous, confusing, or
  misleading information to interfere with surveillance and data
  collection."*
- Six goals of an obfuscation project: *"buying some time, providing
  cover, deniability, evading observation, interfering with profiling, and
  expressing protest."*
- Design dimensions: *"individual or collective, known or unknown,
  selective or general, short-term or long-term."*

**Concrete tactics each of which could be a lab exercise:**

- **Chaff** (§1.1): flooding a detection system with plausible echoes.
- **Twitter bots** (§1.2): filling a signal channel with noise.
- **CacheCloak** (§1.3): request many possible futures so the adversary
  cannot tell which one you chose.
- **TrackMeNot** (§1.4): interleaving real queries with plausible fake
  ones.
- **WikiLeaks upload noise** (§1.5): dummy traffic to hide real
  submissions.
- **False tells** (§1.6): engineered misleading patterns.
- **Group identity / "I am Spartacus"** (§1.7): many people under one
  name. Guy Fawkes / Anonymous already live here.
- **Identical confederates** (§1.8): the Magritte-suited mob in *Thomas
  Crown Affair*. Already framed as art in the text.

**For critique:** each of these is a ready-made conceptual shape for a
project. A student can *literally* make chaff, or a TrackMeNot analog, or
a Spartacus piece, and be doing the course's reversal move in a form the
readings endorse.

### Diederichsen, *On (Surplus) Value In Art* (Week 9)

File: `Diedrich Diederichsen - On (Surplus) Value In Art-Sternberg Press _ Witte de With (2008).md`.

**Why it matters:** the *punch-line* critique. Directly applicable to
prompt-based generative practice, where the prompt *is* the punch line.

- On the demand that art deliver a *Mehrwert* / payoff: *"Since
  Duchamp, the goal of art has been to deliver a punch line, that one
  crucial maneuver, that little extra inspiration. This 'extra
  inspiration' is made up of … the communicative strategies of
  advertising, for which it is important that a brand, a product, and a
  campaign be organized around a single, identifiable, but surprising
  'claim'."* (p. ~4)
- *"The common denominator in this ugly synthesis is the demand for
  punch lines and Mehrwert."* (p. ~5)

**For critique:** the prototype-at-Week-8 checkpoint is the punch-line
test. If the piece compresses to one sentence ("AI Donald Trump as a
cowboy"), it hasn't escaped the *Mehrwert* trap.

### C. Thi Nguyen, *Games: Agency as Art* (Week 12)

File: `C. Thi Nguyen - from Games_ Agency As Art-Oxford University Press (2020).md`.

**Why it matters:** gives the *goal / purpose* distinction and the
*striving play* concept. Useful when students make game-engine work (Week
6), but also broadly for any AI project that has optimization targets.

- *"Games are a method for inscribing forms of agency into artifactual
  vessels … we can be fluid with our agency; we can submerge ourselves in
  alternate agencies designed by another."* (ch. 1)
- *"Striving play … we take up a goal for the sake of the activity of
  struggling for it."* (ch. 1)
- *Suits's portable definition:* *"Playing a game is the voluntary
  attempt to overcome unnecessary obstacles."*

**For critique:** a student whose AI piece "succeeds" at its prompt is an
*achievement player*. A student who imposes unnecessary obstacles on the
tool is a *striving player*. The lab wants the second.

### Natasha Dow Schüll, *Addiction by Design* (Week 7)

File: `Natasha Dow Schüll, from Addiction by Design. Machine Gambling in Las Vegas.md`.

**Why it matters:** the sharpest available picture of what captology
actually looks like when fully industrialized. The target our reversal
move is aimed at.

- *"The thing people never understand is that I'm not playing to win. …
  To keep playing — to stay in that machine zone where nothing else
  matters."* (p. 2, Mollie)
- Industry term: *"time-on-device (TOD)."* (p. 3)
- *"Modern slot machines … are symphonies of individual technologies that
  come together to create a single experience."* (p. 4)
- *"They have become the central nervous system of the casino."* (p. 4)

**For critique:** social feeds, recommendation engines, generative loops,
and gacha game design are all TOD-optimized. Week 7's reversal move is
making something whose aesthetic failure mode is *the user leaves and
comes back in a week*, not *the user can't stop scrolling*.

## Tier B — read but less directly lab-structuring

### Stephen Zepke, *Art as Abstract Machine* (Week 8)

File: `Stephen Zepke, Introduction, Art as Abstract Machine…md`.

**Why it matters:** the strongest philosophical claim in the course for
what art *is*. Language is Deleuzian and will alienate art students
without prior exposure; I'm keeping the text for seminar but not building
the lab on it.

- *"The abstract machine doesn't represent anything because nothing
  exists outside of its action, it is what it does and its immanence is
  always active … it's a means, a vector of creation."*
- *"There is a necessary joy in creation, art is necessarily a liberation
  that explodes everything."*
- *"No creation without destruction."*

**For critique:** if a student connects with Deleuze, this is their text.
Otherwise, Duchamp covers the same ground in prose they can read without
three years of French theory.

### Benzel, *Notes on New Memory* (Week 3, own writing)

File: `Notes on New Memory - Scott Benzel.md`.

**Why it matters:** the "genres are retrospectively assembled" argument.
Takes pressure off students who feel they need to know what their work
*is* before making it.

- *"Dungeon Synth … exists due to the will of its fans who are uncovering
  more and more releases, which in turn, reshapes its history over and
  over."*
- *"Most genres and microgenres are, to greater or lesser degrees,
  retrospective: diverse works are grouped together by fans and critics
  based on shared formal and concept characteristics."*
- *"As we retrospectively reconfigure or attempt to reclaim the past, new
  assemblages come into being while certainties are lost … Are we not
  ourselves retrospectively made?"*

**For critique:** a piece does not need to know what genre it belongs to.
The work is made; meaning accumulates later.

### Benzel, *Notes on Polybius by Sinneslöschen, Nitro Zeus* (Week 5, own writing)

File: `Notes on Polybius by Sinneslöschen, Nitro Zeus - Scott Benzel.md`.

**Why it matters:** legitimizes art-as-rumor / art-as-vaporware as a
permitted project form. A student who wants to make a thing that does not
actually exist but circulates anyway has Benzel's cover.

- *"In tragedy the plausible takes precedence, even if it is false so as
  to deceive the spectators, while in history the truth takes precedence
  to benefit the hearer."* (Polybius, epigraph)
- *"Polybius by Sinnesloschen was a mythic 1981 arcade game/psyop of
  urban legend/RumINT circulating online for years before showing up (as
  recreation/simulation) in gaming and popular culture."*
