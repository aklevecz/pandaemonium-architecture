# Conceptual Frame for the Lab

The lab organizes around four moves a maker can take in relation to the
apparatus of AI/ML. Each is anchored in a course text. None is invented.

> A reminder for all four: the seminar is the place for full theoretical
> argument. The lab page can state these as one-paragraph stances and trust
> students to encounter them more deeply in seminar (or not — readings are
> not required).

---

## I. Apparatus, not tool

> *"The apparatus of AI forms feedback loops with the rest of society:
> it's 'a structured structure that becomes a structuring structure'."*
> — Dan McQuillan, *Resisting AI* (course homepage epigraph)

> *"Apparatuses are not passive observing instruments; on the contrary,
> they are productive of (and part of) phenomena … apparatuses are
> boundary-making practices that are formative of matter and meaning."*
> — Karen Barad, *Meeting the Universe Halfway*, p. 146 (Week 5 reading)

A tool is something you pick up to do work that already had a shape. An
apparatus is something that already has cuts in it before you arrive — it
has decided what counts as a "good" output, what counts as the same image,
what counts as a person, what counts as noise. When a student opens Runway
or Cursor or ChatGPT, they're entering a working environment that has
already enacted those cuts on their behalf.

**The first move, every week, is naming the cuts that the apparatus made
before the student touched it.** Not "what does this tool do" but "what has
this tool already decided is true."

This anchors the entire lab. Students who never read another word of theory
should be able to leave the room able to ask: *what did this thing decide
before I arrived?*

## II. The creative act

> *"Let us consider … the two poles of the creation of art: the artist on
> the one hand, and on the other the spectator who later becomes the
> posterity. … This 'art coefficient' is a personal expression of art 'à
> l'état brut,' that is, still in raw state which must be 'refined' … by
> the spectator … [who] experiences the phenomenon of transmutation;
> through the change from inner matter into a work of art, an actual
> transubstantiation has taken place, and the role of the spectator is to
> determine the weight of the work on the aesthetic scale."*
> — Marcel Duchamp, *The Creative Act* (1957), via Benzel's Week 1 note

Duchamp's premise: the artist makes raw material; the spectator's
encounter is what turns it into a work. Art is a two-pole feedback loop.

For an AI lab this is doubly useful. There are *two* loops: one between
the maker and the apparatus (the model is in the loop with the artist
during making), and one between the work and the viewer (the viewer is in
the loop with the work after making). Most student work fails on the
second loop. The output gets generated, it never gets seen with intention,
and the "art coefficient" never closes.

**Implication for the lab: every milestone has a spectator step.** The
work exists when someone other than the maker has been put into a loop
with it.

## III. Loading the machine

> *"This motionless diagram, with its nine capital letters … is already
> a thinking machine. It was natural for its inventor — a man, we must
> not forget, of the thirteenth century — to feed it with a subject
> matter that now strikes us as unrewarding. … We would load the machine
> differently, no doubt with the words Entropy, Time, Electrons,
> Potential Energy, Fourth Dimension, Relativity, Protons, Einstein. Or
> with Surplus Value, Proletariat, Capitalism, Class Struggle,
> Dialectical Materialism, Engels."*
> — Borges, *Ramón Llull's Thinking Machine*, in Benzel's Week 2 note

> *"It might act upon other things besides number, were objects found
> whose mutual relations could be expressed by those of the abstract
> science of operations."*
> — Ada Lovelace, Note A on the Analytical Engine, in Benzel's Week 2
> note

The apparatus is a combinatorial. The artistic decision is what you put
into it. Llull fed his wheels with thirteenth-century theological
abstractions; Borges proposes feeding the same machine with Marxism or
modern physics; the device is the same; the result is unrecognizably
different.

For art students this is the lever, because the lab is *not* about
training models or writing kernels. It is about *what corpus, what
images, what histories, what voices, what refusals* enter the machine.

A prompt is not craft. The choice of corpus is craft. The choice of what
to refuse to load is craft. **The lab teaches choosing what to load.**

## IV. Ritual of reversal / zones of opacity

> *"…rituals of reversal … 'rewire' the crucial connections in the social
> structure by providing symbolic statements of traditional social
> imperatives and basic categories of … world view."*
> — Evon Z. Vogt, in *Rituals of Reversal* (course homepage epigraph)

> *"Establishing a zone of opacity where people can circulate and
> experiment freely without bringing in the Empire's information flows,
> means producing 'anonymous singularities,' recreating the conditions
> for a possible experience, an experience which will not be immediately
> flattened out by a binary machine assigning a meaning/direction to it.
> … The important thing is maybe to create vacuoles of non-communication,
> interrupters who escape control. Yes, the important thing for us is to
> have opacity zones, opening cavities, empty intervals, black blocs
> within the cybernetic matrix of power."*
> — Tiqqun, *The Cybernetic Hypothesis* (introductory reading)

> *"Obfuscation is the deliberate addition of ambiguous, confusing, or
> misleading information to interfere with surveillance and data
> collection."*
> — Brunton & Nissenbaum, *Obfuscation* (Week 11 reading)

The reversal move is not "use the tool wrong." It is using the tool
toward its inverse aim. AI tools are built for fluency, throughput,
clarity, engagement, capture, and prediction. The reversal move makes
work that is dysfluent, slow, opaque, disengaging, evasive, or
unpredictable — *using the same apparatus.* This is the strongest
political move available in the lab and it is also the most aesthetically
productive, because the apparatus is so heavily optimized for one set of
outputs that any sustained inversion produces strange, unfamiliar work.

Brunton & Nissenbaum give a working catalog of reversal tactics — chaff,
TrackMeNot, CacheCloak, false tells, group identity ("I am Spartacus"),
identical confederates. Each of these is a possible lab exercise or
project mode for art students. They are *also already framed as art* in
the source: the Magritte-suited mob in *Thomas Crown Affair*, the
Anonymous Guy Fawkes mask, "I am Spartacus." The reversal vocabulary is
already an art vocabulary.

## How the four hold together

The four are sequential as stances but parallel as practice. A single
piece of student work can be analyzed through all four:

1. *What did the apparatus decide before you arrived?* (Apparatus)
2. *What did you choose to put into it?* (Loading)
3. *Did you take its native use or invert it?* (Reversal)
4. *Who was the spectator and what did they complete?* (Creative Act)

These are also the four questions a critique can ask. They are the
ground for everything in `02-lab-arc.md`.

## What I deliberately did not include

- **Pandaemonium-as-curriculum metaphor** (each week adds a "demon"). I
  used this in an earlier draft. It's mine, not Selfridge's, and it
  flatters the structure rather than serving it. Dropped.
- **"Practice as critique."** Plausible but ungrounded in the readings.
  Dropped.
- **Three-phase Tools/Media/Project arc.** Pattern-matched off the
  one-line lab descriptions. Doesn't survive contact with the readings.
  Dropped in favor of the four-stance frame above.
- **Abstract Machine as the organizing metaphor.** Zepke is brilliant
  but Deleuzian — the language ("becoming," "atheist mysticism,"
  "deterritorialization") will alienate students who haven't read
  Deleuze, and readings aren't required. Kept as a Week 8 anchor for
  students who want to go deeper, not as the lab's frame.
